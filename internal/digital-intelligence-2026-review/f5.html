Glossary.

  • OSINT (Open-Source Intelligence) — intelligence produced from publicly accessible, openly-available sources (web, social, public records, breach corpora, geospatial, technical data), collected and exploited without privileged network access, a warrant, or field hardware.1
  • WEBINT (Web Intelligence) — the web-resident subset of OSINT: data collected from the internet (surface, deep, and dark web) and processed toward an investigative objective; in vendor usage, often the umbrella term for an integrated web-collection-plus-exploitation platform.2
  • SOCMINT (Social Media Intelligence) — a subset of OSINT restricted to social-platform data: the collection and analysis of posts, networks, and metadata (timestamps, geolocation) to reveal information about individuals, groups, or trends.3

5.1 The Playing Ground

5.1 The Playing Ground

Open-source intelligence is the discipline of gathering and exploiting information that is already in the open. The sources are the web, social platforms, public records, breach corpora, satellite imagery, and the technical exhaust of the internet itself. The work is turning that raw material into something an analyst can act on. One vendor glossary in this exact market defines OSINT as "the process of gathering, analyzing, and utilizing information from publicly accessible sources." It enumerates those sources as "social media platforms, websites and blogs, public government data, satellite imagery, public databases, academic publications and gray literature, technical data (WHOIS, DNS, Certificate Transparency), news and media outlets, public forums and online communities, leaked data, geospatial data, publicly available corporate documents, [and the] dark web."4 The buyer is a national-intelligence or national-security agency, a law-enforcement investigations unit, a defense or military-intelligence cell, a corporate-security or threat-intelligence function, or a financial-crime and due-diligence team.

The defining property of this front — the line that keeps the chapter coherent — is the source being open and web-resident. Access is unprivileged: a vendor or its customer can reach the source without a court order, a carrier tap, or a deployed device. That single property separates OSINT from every other front in this report, and it is worth stating the boundary precisely, because "open-source intelligence" is the front most prone to category sprawl. The word "open" gets stretched to cover anything that touches the internet, and the result is double-counting.

What this front IS. Collection and exploitation of openly-available, web-resident, social, and dark-web data, where the access is unprivileged. The core product is the open-source collection-and-exploitation engine: the scrapers, the dark-web crawlers, the social harvesters, the multilingual entity extraction over web text. The collector is graded here, in Open-Source Intelligence.

What this front is NOT. Three boundaries matter.

First, it is not intercepted content. Three other fronts depend on privileged access to a communications channel: the mass network collection of the National Agencies front, the warrant-scoped lawful interception of the Law Enforcement Monitoring Centers front, and the field radio-frequency collection of the Tactical Intelligence front. OSINT touches only what is openly broadcast or published. The bright line is the privilege of access, not the subject matter. A vendor can detect a threat actor's chatter on a public Telegram channel and stay firmly inside Open-Source Intelligence; intercepting that same actor's private call requires the machinery of the intercept fronts. Same target, different front, because the access right differs.

Second, it is not the fusion layer that ingests OSINT. An OSINT collector belongs to Open-Source Intelligence; a fusion platform that ingests OSINT belongs to the Fusion / Decision Intelligence front, with an Open-Source Intelligence reference. The test that keeps these apart is whether the collection or the analytics-over-supplied-data is the primary product. A vendor whose primary value is the open-source collection engine is graded here. A vendor whose primary value is multi-source analytics on top of feeds the customer supplies — link analysis, case management, cross-source correlation — is graded in the fusion front and merely referenced here. This is the single most important grading discipline for this front. Every serious OSINT vendor also markets analytics, so the only question that resolves the ambiguity is which half of the product the buyer is actually paying for.

Third, it is not generic cyber threat-intelligence that is not open-source-derived. Malware telemetry, honeypot data, proprietary sensor networks, and vendor-internal sinkhole data are threat intelligence but not OSINT — they are proprietary collection. Where a vendor blends OSINT with proprietary feeds, only the open-source-derived portion is the Open-Source Intelligence capability; the rest is adjacent. The heavy overlap between "OSINT platform" and "cyber threat-intelligence platform" is exactly why this front's vendor universe overflows. The front's problem is not finding enough contenders to grade. It is cutting the field down to the ones whose open-source collection is genuinely the product.

Three buyer misconceptions

Three buyer misconceptions

"OSINT is free — it's all on Google." The public-source raw material is free; the differentiated product is everything that sits on top of it. Collection at scale, dark-web access, multilingual coverage, de-duplication and entity-resolution, and a provenance trail are what a buyer actually pays for. Babel Street, for example, markets searching "across thousands of sources... in over 200 languages with citations and data provenance."5 The citations and the provenance are the product, not the raw posts. There is a real free floor here: community-edition tools and open-source toolkits, and increasingly general-purpose large language models running ad-hoc collection.6 But that floor only compresses the low end of basic open-web collection. It pushes commercial vendors up-stack, toward the capabilities the free tools cannot match.

"OSINT and SOCMINT are the same thing." Social-media intelligence is a subset of OSINT, restricted to social platforms. OSINT spans the entire open web, public records, technical data, breach corpora, and geospatial sources.2 3 A vendor strong in social collection is not automatically strong in dark-web or breach-data collection; they are different disciplines with different access problems.

"If it's public, collecting it is legal." Public visibility does not equal lawful collection. Platform terms-of-service, anti-circumvention rules, and data-protection law all constrain how public data may be gathered — and the constraint has teeth, as the scraping litigation below shows.

The data and collection stack

The data and collection stack

The front has two strata: a collection-and-source layer, and an exploitation-and-analytics layer that turns raw open data into structured intelligence. Almost all the differentiation lives in which sources a vendor can reach and how well it exploits them.

On the collection side, the source classes line up roughly by how hard they are to reach.

The surface web — indexed news, blogs, corporate sites, forums, public-records portals — is the cheapest and most contested layer. Everyone collects it, and this is where scraping legality bites. Technical data (WHOIS, DNS, Certificate Transparency) sits here too, as a distinct "technical OSINT" discipline.

The deep web — content behind forms, logins, paywalls, or simply not indexed — is reachable without special privilege, but it requires per-source connectors and query automation. Deep-web breadth — the number of gated-but-public databases a vendor has built connectors for — is a quiet moat that is hard to verify from outside. I'd hypothesize that vendors' "thousands of sources" claims gesture at this connector inventory, though, as the collection-breadth axis below notes, the figure is not independently verifiable.

The dark web — Tor and I2P forums, ransomware leak sites, illicit marketplaces, closed channels — is a specialist discipline requiring persona management, access maintenance, and operational tradecraft. Vendors anchor here explicitly, with Cybersixgill describing automated collection "from dark web forums, marketplaces, and social media platforms,"7 Flashpoint marketing intelligence across "Dark Web, Malware, Vulnerability, OSINT, Financial, Domain, Identity" classes,8 and Fivecast publishing a dedicated dark-web collection methodology.9

The social layer (SOCMINT) — posts, networks, metadata, sentiment — is the most legally exposed modality, for reasons the scraping case below makes plain.

Breach and leaked data — credential dumps and leaked databases circulating in cybercrime channels — is a recognized OSINT source class4 used for identity-resolution and exposure monitoring. It carries the heaviest data-protection and provenance risk of any source here.

Multilingual content — coverage of non-Latin scripts and low-resource languages — is a hard collection-and-exploitation problem that vendors market as a primary discriminator. Babel Street positions on processing "any publicly available input in hundreds of languages."5 For a national-security buyer whose targets operate in Arabic, Farsi, Russian, Mandarin, and regional dialects, language coverage is not a feature line. It is a gating capability.

On the exploitation side, the analytics that stays inside an Open-Source Intelligence product — as distinct from a standalone fusion platform — runs to a handful of functions. They are collection orchestration and de-duplication, multilingual entity extraction and resolution, identity-graph construction across selectors, anomaly and network analysis over social data, and alerting on standing queries. The 2026 inflection adds large-language-model-assisted summarization and natural-language investigation. The grading test holds: this analytics stays inside Open-Source Intelligence when it operates over the vendor's own open-source collection, and tips into the fusion front only when it is sold standalone over customer-supplied feeds.

The six axes buyers discrimina...

The six axes buyers discriminate on

Six dimensions separate the serious vendors from the rest, and each maps to a real, publicly observable cost-and-capability structure.

  1. Language and translation coverage. The breadth of languages a vendor can collect, translate, and entity-resolve — not merely keyword-match. The public proxy is the vendor's own stated language count tied to provenance (Babel Street's "over 200 languages"5). This is the hardest-to-fake axis: keyword translation is cheap, but cross-script entity-resolution at investigative grade is not. I read a vendor's willingness to publish a specific language count tied to provenance as a stronger signal than a generic "multilingual" claim.
  1. Dark and deep-web depth. Reach into Tor and I2P forums, ransomware leak sites, and gated-but-public databases — plus the tradecraft to maintain access. The public proxy is a published collection methodology and explicit source-class enumeration8 9 versus generic "we monitor the dark web" marketing. This is where the cyber-threat-intel cohort out-differentiates the social-first WEBINT cohort.
  1. Collection breadth. The raw count and diversity of connected sources across surface, deep, social, dark, breach, and records. The public proxy is the "thousands of sources" claim — directionally informative but not independently verifiable, so they read as positioning claims, not facts, unless tied to a published source inventory.
  1. LLM-native versus bolt-on AI — as positioning, not verified architecture. An outside party cannot verify a vendor's true internal architecture. So this axis must be read as how a vendor markets itself, with a falsifiable test on public proxies. The 2026 market is visibly bifurcating between vendors retrofitting generative AI onto an existing collection stack and vendors positioning AI as the native interface. Babel Street markets "agentic AI" explicitly: "Insights Investigator is agentic AI that gives analysts a single natural language interface to run threat investigations, monitor emerging risks, and surface connections" and "plans and executes multi-step workflows."10 I read "agentic AI" and "natural language interface" as the current category-defining positioning move in this front — not yet a verified architectural moat. The test runs through the second half of 2026. If the agentic-OSINT entrants publish named government reference wins or independent (not vendor-asserted) time-to-insight benchmarks, the LLM-native claim graduates from positioning to a defensible differentiator. If they do not, it stays a watchlist item rather than a claim the analysis can lean on.
  1. Identity-graph and correlation depth. How deeply a vendor resolves selectors — an email, a phone, a handle, an image — into unified entities, and maps relationships across sources. This is the difference between "here are forty hits for this handle" and "here is the resolved person, their aliases, their network, with provenance on each edge." The public proxy is published case studies and the presence of cross-source correlation in product descriptions, versus single-source search.
  1. Data provenance and defensibility. Whether collected intelligence carries an auditable chain — source, timestamp, citation — that survives legal and evidentiary scrutiny, and whether the collection method itself is defensible (terms-of-service-compliant, not fake-account scraping). The public proxy is explicit "citations and data provenance" positioning5 and, inversely, litigation exposure. I read provenance and defensibility as having flipped from a compliance checkbox to a competitive axis. A buyer that adopts a vendor whose collection method is legally contestable inherits that vendor's litigation risk. So defensible collection is now a sales differentiator, not just a hygiene item.

The public barriers

The public barriers

Two structural barriers are most specific to this front, and one — scraping legality — has no analog anywhere else in this report.

Export controls on surveillance technology. Open-source tooling sold for surveillance use sits inside the dual-use export-control net. That net gates who a vendor may sell to across borders. The multilateral baseline is the 42-state Wassenaar Arrangement, which added commercial surveillance technologies to its dual-use control list beginning in 2013. The precise placement matters and is often mis-stated. In the implementing regimes, intrusion-software controls fall under Category 4 (Computers), while IP-network communications surveillance systems fall under Category 5 Part 1 (Telecommunications) — not both under "Category 5," as the shorthand sometimes has it.11

The U.S. implementation came via a Bureau of Industry and Security interim final rule published on 2021-10-21. It controls two categories. The first is software "specially designed... for the generation, command and control, or delivery of 'intrusion software'" under ECCN 4D004. The second is "IP network communications surveillance systems or equipment" under ECCN 5A001.j. Both are controlled for national-security and anti-terrorism reasons, and a new License Exception ACE (Authorized Cybersecurity Exports) carves out certain destinations.12

The European Union's Dual-Use Regulation 2021/821 adds an Article 5 catch-all on cyber-surveillance items. It requires an export authorization where the items "are or may be intended for use in connection with internal repression and/or the commission of serious violations of human rights." Covered technologies named in the guidance include "telecommunications interception systems, internet surveillance systems, intrusion software and forensic tools."13 One honest qualifier: a public analysis from SIPRI finds that "use of the new catch-all control has been limited to date"13 — the control exists on paper, but enforcement practice is still maturing.

Pure open-source collection over public data sits at the lighter-touch end of this net relative to intrusion and implant tooling. But the export-control frame supplies the front's legitimacy boundary, and it is the natural place to seat the surveillance-vendor discussion. One discipline binds throughout. Any certification or clearance claim about a named vendor must cite the certifying body or the vendor's own public statement, never an analyst assertion — a wrong certification claim on a named company is a credibility and a defamation hit.

Platform terms-of-service and scraping legality. This barrier has no analog in the intercept fronts, because this front collects from third-party platforms whose terms bind the collector. The clearest public case is Meta v. Voyager Labs. Meta sued Voyager in January 2023 in the Northern District of California, alleging that Voyager "created 38,000 Facebook accounts" and used them "to scrape data from more than 600,000 social media users," including "posts, likes, friend lists, photos, and comments."14 In May 2024, U.S. District Judge Araceli Martinez-Olguin allowed the suit to proceed, rejecting Voyager's motion to dismiss and finding that Meta's allegations could support claims that Voyager violated Facebook's and Instagram's terms of service.15 The case ultimately settled, with Voyager agreeing "to refrain from scraping Facebook and Instagram and to permanently delete any data collected from Meta."16

Voyager is in this report's never-graded set, so this is descriptive market structure only, not a pattern claim about the company. But the structure it reveals is real: a major platform pursued, and a court allowed to proceed, terms-based claims against an OSINT vendor for collecting publicly visible data through fake accounts, and the matter ended in a settlement that barred the collection. I read the case as the public market's clearest signal that collection method — not just what data is public — is now a structural constraint on this front, and that platform enforcement is a live competitive variable. The test runs through the second half of 2026. If additional platform-versus-vendor enforcement actions surface in the public record as named filings or settlements, the "defensible collection" axis hardens into a table-stakes procurement question. If enforcement stays a one-off, it remains an elevated-risk axis rather than a gate.

Behind the single case sits a genuinely unsettled legal backdrop across jurisdictions — U.S. terms-breach theories, EU lawful-basis and purpose-limitation constraints on reusing personal data, platform anti-circumvention rules. The absence of a settled rule is itself the barrier: each vendor must make a defensibility bet, and that bet is now visible in both its positioning and its litigation exposure.

Clearance and accreditation. For U.S.-federal and allied national-security buyers, the software-side gates that recur across this report apply here too: facility and personnel security clearances, and cloud accreditation (FedRAMP authorization, DoD Impact Levels) for hosted delivery. I did not extract a verbatim accreditation-requirement line from a live OSINT-specific U.S. solicitation in this pass. So the specific magnitude and timeline of the gate is an inference from the regime's general applicability rather than a quoted fact — a thin spot worth naming as one. The war-chest analysis later in this chapter is better placed to answer it, through the pattern of specialists acquiring cleared U.S. delivery organizations.

One freshness correction for the contenders that follow. As of a 2026-06-14 web check, Cobwebs Technologies operates under PenLink, both held by the private-equity firm Spire Capital, with the Cobwebs-to-Spire transaction reported at roughly USD 200 million. PenLink is integrating Cobwebs' web-intelligence capability into its digital-investigation platform for "law enforcement, public safety, and national security customers."17 Babel Street does not own PenLink or Cobwebs — those sit under Spire Capital, and Babel Street is a separate vendor.17 The contenders and war-chest sections re-verify any 2026 ownership before grading.

The government slice: a labeled proxy, not a segment size

No clean public dollar figure isolates the government and national-security OSINT software slice. Given the opacity of government intelligence budgets, that is an expected, honest result, not a gap to be filled by invention. What is publicly stated is the government share of the OSINT market by named publishers, which I carry strictly as a proxy — a share-of-market, not a standalone segment size. Mordor puts government intelligence agencies at 38.40% of 2025 OSINT revenue, the single largest end-user segment, with military and defense as a further distinct category.18 Global Market Insights puts national security at 32% of 2025 market value, the dominant application segment, covering "counterterrorism, counter-intelligence, strategic warning and foreign capability assessment."20

Applying those published shares to the publishers' own market totals is bracketing arithmetic, and I label it as exactly that, not as a publisher figure. Mordor's 38.40% government share of its USD 18.20B total implies an order-of-magnitude proxy near USD 7B for the government-intelligence OSINT slice in 2025. GMI's 32% national-security share of its USD 12.7B total implies near USD 4B. The proxy range — roughly USD 4–7B for 2025 — is arithmetic on published application-share splits, not a methodology-disclosed segment sizing, and no publisher sells the latter. The harder currency for this front is not any market-derivative at all; it is the named, dated procurement evidence below.

Demand drivers

Demand drivers

Three forcing functions carry the analytical weight that the market numbers cannot.

The open-data exhaust has outrun manual analysis. The publishers converge on a single root driver: the volume of openly-available data has grown faster than any analyst team can read it. Global Market Insights frames demand around the "rapid increase in publicly available online data" across "blogs, social media sites, news sites, web forums, internet of things (IoT) devices."20 Multilingual scale is a named buyer requirement rather than a feature. Babel Street states publicly that it analyzes content across "over 200 languages," finding connections in multilingual data "from social media, the deep web, and public records."23 (Dataminr's own filing-grade description cites "trillions of daily computations across billions of public data inputs from over 100,000 unique public data sources." It spans "global and regional social media platforms, blogs, web forums... the deep and dark web, cyber signals, and public IoT sensors" — a vivid statement of the source span. But it dates to a 2021-03-23 press release, so I carry it as a description of breadth, not as a current metric.24) Dark and deep-web depth is a distinct vector the publishers single out, with The Business Research Company defining part of its OSINT market as "dark web analysis" revenue.19 Every expansion of the open-data exhaust — more platforms, more languages, more dark-web venues — widens the gap between what is publicly knowable and what a human can manually process. That gap is precisely what commercial platforms monetize.

Government buyers are institutionalizing OSINT as a named discipline. The U.S. intelligence community has publicly committed to OSINT as a standing mission rather than an ad-hoc capability. The CIA has publicly released an OSINT strategy that calls for building data-science and AI talent and explicitly enlists large language models in its OSINT mission.25 The buyer set the publishers name is broad and government-weighted — government intelligence agencies, military and defense, federal, state, and local law enforcement, plus financial-services and corporate-security threat-intel teams.18 20 Recorded Future's own public client claim — "more than 1,900 clients across 75 countries, including the governments of 45 countries" — is a public datapoint on the government density of the buyer base.26

A commercial base sits below the government buyers. The same publishers that put government at 32–38% of revenue also show financial services, cybersecurity providers, and corporate-risk firms as material end-user segments.18 20 I read this as the structurally important feature of this front relative to the classified-collection fronts. OSINT has a large commercial recurring-revenue base — the part that needs no warrant and no clearance — which several specialists use as the platform from which they attack government tenders. If the named OSINT specialists' public disclosures show government revenue growing materially faster than commercial through 2027, the "commercial base funds the government push" read weakens.

The AI and LLM shift

The AI and LLM shift

The clearest 2025–2026 public signal in this front is that the incumbents are re-positioning around generative and agentic AI to compress the analyst workflow. The discipline here is the same one the differentiation axis above demands: this is framed strictly as what vendors publicly claim, because true architecture cannot be verified from outside — "positions itself as," not "is."

The capital is explicitly chasing it. Dataminr raised USD 100M from Fortress Investment Group in April 2025 "to accelerate its development of generative and agentic AI products," following USD 85M (March 2025) from NightDragon and HSBC.27 I flag the "$4.1B valuation" figure that circulates with Dataminr as the 2021 Series F mark, not a current valuation;24 the 2025 raises are convertible or credit, not a fresh primary equity mark.27 Babel Street publicly markets "agentic AI for accelerated insights into global security," with "multilingual data summarization, pattern identification, and social connection analysis" as its AI workflow claim.28 Palantir positions its AIP — using "generative and agentic AI models, including large language models (LLMs)" inside Gotham and Foundry, deployed to government from 2023 — as the operationalization layer over intelligence data.29 Palantir's relevance to this front, though, is as the fusion layer that ingests OSINT, not as a pure collector. And Fivecast, the Australian specialist, raised an AUD 30M Series A for an "AI and machine-learning-powered" platform scanning "social media and the dark web."30

The shared public claim across these vendors is that large-language-model and agentic AI compress the time from raw public-data ingestion to analyst-ready insight. The observable proxies are funding explicitly earmarked for generative or agentic AI (Dataminr's April-2025 round states this purpose verbatim), public product-page repositioning toward "agentic," and named government reference wins. If the AI-native entrants cannot be named from public pages with a public capability claim, this trend demotes from a central convergence pillar to a watchlist item. For now, several of them can be named, which is what keeps it a live candidate.

The In-Q-Tel signal. In-Q-Tel, the intelligence community's non-profit strategic investor, "funded primarily by the Central Intelligence Agency," is the public signal of which commercial data and AI capabilities the community is steering toward. Its portfolio spans data and AI infrastructure broadly, and pinning a specific OSINT thesis on it from public sources would over-claim.

Consolidation as a market-structure force. The 2024–2025 deal flow shows OSINT and intelligence assets being absorbed into larger platforms. Mastercard acquired Recorded Future for USD 2.65B from Insight Partners (announced September 2024), framing it as "the world's largest threat intelligence company."26 35 Cobwebs Technologies, the Israeli WEBINT vendor, joined PenLink after being acquired for roughly USD 200M by Spire Capital, the private-equity firm that also owns PenLink — the deal that defines the current PenLink/Cobwebs entity referenced in this report.36 That single-entity status, and Babel Street's current ownership, are flagged for re-confirmation when the contenders are graded.

The scraping and privacy vector — asymmetric, and the front's defining medium-term risk. OSINT's raw material is openly-available data, which puts it squarely inside the 2024–2026 tightening of data-broker and scraping law. That is a real force, but an asymmetric one, because national-security and law-enforcement use is frequently carved out.

On the commercial edge, scraping of public personal data is under sustained legal pressure. Clearview AI's facial-image scraping drew more than EUR 91M in fines across 15 jurisdictions by 2025, including a EUR 20M Italian order and a EUR 30.5M Dutch order. It also drew a roughly USD 51M class-action settlement that received final approval on 2025-03-20. The regulatory backdrop is one in which EU authorities have held that "public availability is not a sufficient lawful basis under GDPR."37 U.S. enforcement tightened and then partly reversed, with a carve-out running through both moves. The FTC finalized orders in January 2025 against Mobilewalla, Gravy Analytics, and X-Mode/Outlogic barring the sale of sensitive location data — orders that exempt national-security and law-enforcement purposes.38 Meanwhile the CFPB's broader data-broker rule, which would have classed some brokers as FCRA consumer-reporting agencies, was withdrawn on 2025-05-15.39

I read the net regulatory vector as ambiguous-but-tightening on the commercial edge and carved-out on the government edge. Consumer-facing data-broker selling is constrained, while law-enforcement and national-security access is repeatedly exempted, and the most sweeping U.S. broker rule was withdrawn. If a major OSINT or WEBINT vendor loses a government contract or faces a regulator order specifically tied to public-data scraping during 2026–2027, the "government edge is carved out" read weakens. The carve-outs are publicly contested. Citizen Lab's analysis of PenLink's ad-based geolocation and Amnesty International's reporting on Palantir and Babel Street surveillance concerns are the named-outlet evidence of that38, and I surface those only as descriptive public market-force commentary, not as a pattern claim. This legal vector is, to my read, the most likely source of a 2026–2027 discontinuity in this front.

5.3 The Contenders

Mastercard bought Recorded Future — described in the deal release as "the world's largest threat intelligence company, with more than 1,900 clients across 75 countries, including the governments of 45 countries"96 — for $2.65B, announced 12 Sep 2024 and finalized 20 Dec 202497. Bitsight agreed to acquire dark-web specialist Cybersixgill for ~$115M (announced 14 Nov 2024)98. Haveli Investments took ZeroFox private for ~$350M enterprise value at $1.14/share, closed 13 May 202499. PenLink, owned by PE firm Spire Capital, had earlier absorbed Israeli WEBINT vendor Cobwebs Technologies (~$200M, July 2023)100. And Dataminr — itself last priced at $4.1B in 2021 and recapitalized in 2025 via a $100M Fortress convertible rather than a fresh equity mark101 — announced intent to acquire ThreatConnect for $290M on 21 Oct 2025102.My read. I read this as the closing of the independent-OSINT-brand path. The category has become strategic enough that the buyers are no longer other intelligence specialists — they are payments networks (Mastercard), security-ratings platforms (Bitsight), and PE control vehicles (Haveli, Spire) folding collection assets into larger portfolios. The standalone-public route has effectively shut: ZeroFox de-listed, Recorded Future was absorbed into a payments giant, and no fresh standalone primary mega-round on a pure-play collector surfaced in named-outlet reporting across the window. The surviving independents (Babel Street, Flashpoint, Fivecast, Strider) are now either acquisition candidates or vertical specialists narrow enough to be uninteresting to a generalist acquirer — which is its own kind of moat.Conditional prediction. The absorption thesis hardens into the defining structure of the front under one of two triggers: H2 2026 brings a second large OSINT/CTI pure-play acquired by a non-defense platform company (payments, cloud, identity, or ratings), or a further roll-up is announced following the now-closed Dataminr/ThreatConnect deal. Remaining independents should then increasingly be read as acquisition candidates rather than durable standalones. The pull is weaker than the deal flow implied — and independent growth is still financeable — under two inverse signals. Either a pure-play collector raises a fresh, named-lead primary equity mega-round at an up valuation, or Recorded Future's government-sector roster visibly contracts post-close, with a loss of named public-sector references signaling integration strain.Sources. 96 97 98 99 100 101 102Pattern Claim 2 — The Agentic RepositioningObservation. What is observably real is the funding earmarked for it (Dataminr's April-2025 round states the purpose verbatim101) and the simultaneity of the pivot. I read these two facts as a vise on the middle of the market. Because the procurement lane is genuinely accessible — GSA/Carahsoft subscriptions, recompeted enterprise licenses, publicly-litigated protests — a software/data specialist can enter this front without owning sovereign infrastructure. That is exactly why the front overflows with contenders and why it consolidates so fast. But the same accessibility means the low end is being eaten from below by free tooling and commodity LLMs. The vendors who keep pricing power are the ones who climb up-stack to where the free floor cannot follow: multilingual exploitation at investigative grade, dark/deep-web depth with maintained access, defensible provenance, and accredited government deployment. Winners, Watch, Losers

Winners, Watch, Losers

Winners.

  • Recorded Future. Sold at the top of the cycle. The $2.65B exit to Mastercard from PE owner Insight Partners97 is the largest disclosed transaction in the front. It is the cleanest "winning" outcome available to a pure-play: a strategic acquirer paid a payments-network premium for the category's most-cited brand. The Winners label is opinion-labeled and scoped to the transaction, not to post-close execution, which the conditional in Pattern Claim 1 flags as the open risk.
  • Dataminr. The lone independent still acting as a consolidator rather than a target: a $100M Fortress financing (Apr 2025)101 funding the agentic build, plus its $290M ThreatConnect acquisition (announced Oct 2025, closed 2025-11-25)102 extending it from real-time alerting toward the fusion/analytics layer. I read Dataminr as winning the "independent that buys rather than gets bought" position in 2026. The caveat is that its last priced mark is the 2021 $4.1B Series F and the 2025 raise was a convertible, not a fresh equity up-round101.
  • Strider Technologies. Winning the net-new vertical, not the incumbent fight. A $55M Series C led by Pelion (Sep 2024, ~$117M total)114 funds a nation-state / economic-espionage wedge — "The Frontier of Strategic Intelligence"105 — that the cyber-CTI generalists do not own. It is winning a budget line, not a market-share war, which is the defensible thing to win when the generalists are consolidating.

Watch.

  • Babel Street. The most active independent build-and-hire of 2026 — "Agentic Risk Intelligence" repositioning104, a federal-and-AI leadership stack (John Larson, ex-Booz Allen, as President and Chief AI Officer, Dec 2025)115, and a Carahsoft/GSA federal channel109. But its ownership and funding could not be cleanly resolved from named-outlet reporting; it is recorded as privately held, ownership undisclosed116. Watch signal: a fresh named-lead primary round (which would test Pattern Claim 1's "independent growth is still financeable" branch) or an acquisition (which would confirm the absorption branch).
  • Fivecast. The clearest government-mission OSINT pure-play in the cut — "Government agencies (40+)," "Law enforcement (30+ police departments)"117 — pursuing a partnership stack rather than M&A to reach a full collect-to-evidence workflow. That is the Tranquility AI collection-plus-evidence partnership, Mar 2026118, on a US$20M Series A base (2023)119. Watch signal: a named government reference win from the partnership, or the partnership stack itself getting acquired into a generalist's portfolio.

Losers.

No graded contender in this front earns a Losers label in this section. A vendor reaches this line only when a cited public event anchors a vendor-specific decline, per the casualty-evidence discipline. The qualifying events are a layoff specific to the OSINT business, a missed quarter, a down-round, a named executive departure framed as distress, or a customer-churn disclosure. The public events on this front are consolidation and strategic exit, not failure. ZeroFox's $1.14/share take-private99 sits well below its SPAC-era levels but is a PE de-listing, not a documented operational collapse; Dataminr's reported 2023 staff reduction120 and Primer's pre-turnaround leadership change121 are descriptive context, not casualty labels. Voyager Labs and the NSO/Candiru/Intellexa/Ability cohort are litigation-sensitive and never graded; they appear only as descriptive market-force commentary elsewhere in the chapter, with no decline label asserted here. Quarterly refreshes will populate this line if an OSINT-specific distress signal emerges on the public record.

5.7 The Campaign Ahead

5.7 The Campaign Ahead

Five watchlist items for H2 2026. Each names a public, observable threshold that would trigger re-assessment of a claim above.

  1. A second non-defense-platform acquisition of an OSINT pure-play. Signal to monitor: M&A announcements naming a payments, cloud, identity, or security-ratings acquirer of a standalone OSINT/CTI collector. Threshold for re-assessment: one such deal announced in H2 2026, or — with the Dataminr/ThreatConnect acquisition already closed (2025-11-25)102 — a further roll-up, or a Dataminr IPO pricing → Pattern Claim 1's absorption thesis hardens into the front's defining structure. Inverse threshold: a fresh named-lead primary equity mega-round at an up valuation on a pure-play collector → independent growth is still financeable. Primary source: named-outlet M&A coverage (Reuters, Bloomberg, Calcalist), vendor press releases.
  1. A named-outcome agentic reference win. Signal: public reference customers attributing a specific decision or outcome to autonomous agentic action, as distinct from analyst-assisted search, from any of Dataminr, Babel Street, Strider, or Cognyte. Threshold: one vendor publishes such a named reference (or an independent time-to-insight benchmark) and the others do not → the agentic claims begin separating into a real differentiator per Pattern Claim 2's first branch. Inverse: "agentic" stays uniform marketing language across all of them through Q4 2026 with no named-outcome win → demote the AI-native convergence pillar to a watchlist label. Primary source: vendor press releases (Dataminr, Babel Street, Strider, Cognyte newsrooms), government reference-win coverage.
  1. A scraping-law action against a government-facing OSINT vendor. Signal: a regulator order or lost government contract specifically tied to public-data scraping affecting a named OSINT/WEBINT vendor. The backdrop is contested — GDPR's "public availability is not a sufficient lawful basis" line and the FTC's national-security-carved-out location-broker orders122. Threshold: one such named action in 2026 → the "government edge is carved out" read in Pattern Claim 3 weakens and the squeeze migrates from the commodity tier into the mission tier. Inverse: scraping-law enforcement stays confined to consumer-facing brokers (e.g., the Clearview-class fines122) → the carve-out holds. Primary source: regulator press (FTC, EU DPAs), named-outlet legal coverage, Citizen Lab / Amnesty public analysis.
  1. Recorded Future's government franchise post-close. Signal: Recorded Future's named public-sector reference roster and any government-contract activity following the Mastercard close (20 Dec 2024)97. Threshold: visible contraction of named public-sector references, or a public-sector contract loss attributable to the acquisition → integration is straining the government franchise (Pattern Claim 1's inverse branch). Conversely, retained or expanded named government references → the strategic acquirer preserved the franchise. Primary source: Recorded Future / Mastercard newsroom, federal procurement records (USAspending), named-outlet coverage.
  1. A generalist answering the nation-state / supply-chain wedge. Signal: whether a generalist (Recorded Future/Mastercard, Dataminr) publicly launches a named "nation-state risk" or "supply-chain / vendor risk" product line answering Strider's strategic-intelligence wedge105. A related signal is whether Babel Street's standing-up of a dedicated threat-and-vendor-risk function115 expands into a named product. Threshold: a generalist ships a named competing line in H2 2026 → the vertical wedge is validated as a category the incumbents must answer (a winning signal for Strider's position). Inverse: Strider's next disclosed round stalls or its messaging reverts to generic threat intel → the wedge was narrower than the raise implied. Primary source: vendor product-launch pages and press releases, funding-round coverage.
