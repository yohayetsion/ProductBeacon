Front-matter glossary.

  • Fusion (data fusion) — the analytical layer that ingests already-collected data from multiple sources and combines it into a single, resolved, relationship-mapped intelligence picture, without itself performing the collection.[^pg1]
  • Decision intelligence (DI) — per Gartner, "a practical discipline that advances decision making by explicitly understanding and engineering how decisions are made, and how outcomes are evaluated, managed and improved via feedback"; a decision-intelligence platform composes "data, analytics, knowledge and artificial intelligence (AI) techniques" to support, automate, and augment decisions.[^pg2]
  • Entity resolution — the process of determining that records across different sources refer to the same real-world entity (a person, organization, device, or account), consolidating them into a single, accurate view that feeds a knowledge graph.[^pg3]
  • Link analysis — the mapping and traversal of relationships between resolved entities — people, organizations, events, transactions — to surface connections, networks, and the significant paths between them, built on an entity-link-property methodology.[^pg4]

4.1 The Playing Ground

4.1 The Playing Ground

Fusion is the front where intelligence stops being about getting the data and starts being about making sense of it. Every other front in this report produces material — intercepted signals, field-collected geolocation, open-source web extractions — and all of that material has to be correlated, de-duplicated, resolved into real-world people and organizations, mapped into relationships, placed in space and time, and turned into something an analyst can act on. That work is fusion. The product an investigative-analytics vendor sells is described, in its own terms, as letting analysts "map and analyze connections between entities such as people, organizations, and events," built on an "entity-link-property (ELP) methodology" that "reveals relationships between data entities to discover patterns."[^pg4] The buyer-facing name the market now uses for the top of this layer is decision intelligence, which Gartner defines as "a practical discipline that advances decision making by explicitly understanding and engineering how decisions are made," delivered through a platform that composes "data, analytics, knowledge and artificial intelligence (AI) techniques."[^pg2] The buyer is a national-intelligence or national-security agency, a military-intelligence fusion cell, a law-enforcement major-investigations or intelligence unit, a financial-intelligence unit, or a cross-agency task force.

The defining property of this front — the line that keeps the chapter coherent — is that it is an analytical layer, not a collection silo. And because fusion by construction touches everything else in the report, the boundary here is the most fragile in the whole study and has to be stated with unusual precision:

Fusion is the analytical LAYER on top of collection, not a peer collection silo; a vendor is graded here ONLY if it sells fusion standalone to bring-your-own-source buyers; collection vendors with built-in dashboards are graded in their collection front and only referenced here.

That sentence does more grading work than any other in the report, so it is worth unpacking what it includes and excludes.

What this front IS. The standalone analytical layer that ingests already-collected data from many sources and turns it into resolved entities, mapped relationships, geospatial and temporal context, and managed investigations — entity resolution, link and graph analysis, geospatial-temporal analysis, investigative case management, cross-source correlation, and, as the 2026 inflection, AI- and LLM-assisted investigation. The platform is source-agnostic by design: it is sold to a bring-your-own-source buyer who already has, or separately procures, the collection. The load-bearing product is the resolution-and-graph engine, not the connection to any one sensor.

What this front is NOT. Three boundaries matter. First, it is not a collection silo with a built-in dashboard. A Monitoring Center that ships an analyst console (F2), a deep-packet-inspection appliance with a profiling UI (F1), an IMSI-catcher with a mapping view (F3), or an OSINT collector with a results graph (F5) is graded in its collection front and only referenced here. The test is whether the vendor sells the fusion layer standalone to buyers who bring their own sources — Palantir, i2, Siren, DataWalk, Nuix, and Cognyte's NEXYTE-as-platform pass that test; a collection box's analytics module does not. This single discipline kills the largest double-counting risk in the report: almost every collection vendor also markets "analytics," and counting those modules as fusion contenders would charge the same revenue and the same product to two chapters. Second, it is not the collection itself. Fusion does not tap a channel, deploy a device, or scrape the web — it consumes the output of the collection fronts. Where a vendor (Cognyte, Cellebrite, BAE) operates in both a collection front and the fusion layer, its fusion grade rests only on its standalone analytics product, re-sourced to a page that markets that product as a platform. Third, it is not general-purpose business intelligence. Tableau-class dashboards and horizontal commercial "decision intelligence" platforms are decision-support software, but the F4 contender set is the investigative, intelligence-grade subset: entity-centric, relationship-first, provenance-aware, and deployable into accredited government environments. The arrival of an inaugural Gartner "Decision Intelligence Platforms" Magic Quadrant[^pg5] validates the category name commercially — but the government-intelligence fusion vendors graded here are a distinct, accreditation-gated cohort. The label overlaps; the buyer and the deployment do not.

Three buyer misconceptions

Three buyer misconceptions

"Fusion is just a nicer dashboard on top of our collection." The hard problem is not visualization. It is entity resolution at scale — deciding that "J. Smith," "Smith, John," and a bare phone number are the same person across millions of records — and relationship inference across heterogeneous sources. Palantir frames the differentiator not as the screen but as the ontology: software that "takes an organization's vast, disparate data and formats it into a common and relatable picture," a "semantic layer that integrates the data, logic, and actions of an institution."[^pg6] The dashboard is the cheap part. The resolution-and-ontology engine is where the switching cost lives — which, as the barriers below show, is exactly why incumbency in this layer is so sticky.

"Whoever collects the data should own the analysis." The market has decisively separated the two. A buyer can run multiple collection vendors — a signals box from one, OSINT from another, lawful interception from a third — into a single fusion platform, and increasingly wants to, precisely to avoid being locked into one vendor's full stack. I read the standalone-fusion vendors as structurally betting against the vertically integrated collection-plus-analytics vendors on exactly this point: source-agnosticism is their wedge, and it is also the grading line that defines who belongs in this chapter.

"Centralizing everything into one data lake is the only way to fuse it." This is the live architectural debate of the front. A growing cohort markets federation — analyzing data where it lives, without bulk-copying it into a central store — as the answer for sovereignty-, classification-, and governance-constrained buyers. Siren's federation capability, for instance, "enables seamless correlation across multiple distributed data sources… without centralizing them."[^pg7] The "you must build the lake first" assumption is precisely what the federated camp contests.

The data and technology stack

The data and technology stack

The stack reads in three strata: an ingestion-and-integration layer that pulls from the collection fronts and normalizes their wildly different outputs; a resolution-and-graph core that turns normalized records into entities and relationships; and an exploitation-and-workflow layer of geospatial-temporal analysis, case management, governed collaboration, and AI-assisted investigation. The differentiation lives in how well a vendor does the core at scale and how openly it does the ingestion.

On ingestion, fusion is downstream of everything. Its inputs are the heterogeneous outputs of the four collection fronts — high-volume signals metadata from F1, evidentiary warrant-scoped intercept product and chain-of-custody from F2, time-and-place-stamped field fixes from F3, variable-reliability open-web and dark-web extractions from F5 — plus the buyer's own case files, watchlists, and identity databases. Vendors consume this through connectors and standard interfaces: Siren ingests "using a variety of tools including JDBC and web services," combining "digital forensic data, metadata from audio and video, and call data records… with internal databases to provide a more complete picture,"[^pg7] while Cognyte's NEXYTE markets that it "Preloads OSINT, BIZINT and commercial data into one intelligence view," fusing "text, image, audio & video files."[^pg8] I read the connector inventory — how many of the collection fronts' formats a fusion vendor ingests out of the box — as a plausible but hard-to-verify moat, and I read "fuses any source" claims as directional positioning rather than a disclosed integration surface, unless a vendor ties the claim to a published connector list.

The resolution-and-graph core is the genuinely hard part. Entity resolution — deciding that records across sources refer to the same real-world entity — is the load-bearing capability, with DataWalk describing a "powerful entity resolution facility to ensure an accurate view of connected, consolidated data" feeding "a single knowledge graph,"[^pg3] and Cognyte marketing "advanced entity resolution" to "resolve identities and uncover relations."[^pg8] Link and graph analysis maps and traverses the relationships between resolved entities, with "finding the shortest and most significant path across connected records (phone calls, messages, social links)" described as "a typical example of a widely used investigative graph algorithm."[^pg7] Geospatial-temporal analysis places entities and events in space and time for pattern-of-life and movement reconstruction.

The exploitation-and-workflow layer is where the analyst actually lives: investigative case management as the system of record for an investigation (Nuix Neo markets "Intelligence, Collaboration & Analytics for Investigations"[^pg9]); governed cross-agency collaboration, where resolved intelligence is "shared across organizational boundaries while maintaining strict governance and permissions"[^pg8] — not a nicety but a deployment prerequisite, because a fusion platform aggregates data of mixed classification and must enforce need-to-know inside one workspace; and AI-assisted investigation, the 2026 inflection, framed throughout as positioning rather than verified architecture.

The axes buyers discriminate o...

The axes buyers discriminate on

Five dimensions separate the serious fusion vendors, each mapping to a real, publicly observable cost-and-capability structure.

  1. Entity-resolution accuracy at scale. The accuracy of the resolution core under real-world volume and ambiguity — the difference between a graph that looks connected and one whose merges an analyst can defend in court. This is the least externally verifiable axis: no vendor publishes false-merge rates. I read a vendor's willingness to tie resolution to an auditable provenance trail per edge as the stronger public signal, and the absence of one as a flag.
  1. Federated versus central-data-lake architecture — the load-bearing wedge. Whether a vendor requires data to be centralized into its own store or can federate — analyze data in place across distributed sources without bulk-copying it. Siren positions explicitly on federation, and the general technical case is that "data federation should be used when data must remain at source due to governance, compliance, sovereignty, or contractual requirements."[^pg7][^pg10] I read federation as a genuine architectural wedge specifically for classification- and sovereignty-constrained government buyers, for whom bulk-copying mixed-classification data into one vendor's lake is itself a security and policy problem, not merely a cost question. If, through H2 2026, federated-architecture vendors publish named government reference wins that explicitly cite data-residency or no-bulk-copy as the selection rationale, the federation wedge hardens from positioning into a procurement-grade discriminator; if those wins do not surface publicly, it stays a marketed architectural preference.
  1. Native-source breadth. How many of the collection fronts' formats, plus the buyer's internal systems, a vendor ingests out of the box, and how much custom integration each new source costs. The public proxy is enumerated data-type support (NEXYTE's "text, image, audio & video" plus "OSINT, BIZINT and commercial"[^pg8]) versus unquantified "all data" claims, which are positioning, not facts, unless tied to a published connector list.
  1. AI- and LLM-assisted investigation — as positioning, not verified architecture. An outside party cannot verify a vendor's true internal architecture, so this axis must be read as how a vendor markets itself, with a falsifiable test on public proxies. The 2026 market is visibly attaching natural-language and agentic layers over the resolved graph — NEXYTE markets that it "Reveals hidden connections with AI and natural language search,"[^pg8] Palantir centers AIP, and the broader framing is Gartner's prediction that "by 2027, 50% of business decisions will be augmented or automated by AI agents for decision intelligence."[^pg2] I read "AI-assisted investigation" as the current category-defining positioning move in fusion — parallel to the OSINT front's "agentic" race — not yet a verified architectural moat. If, by H2 2026, the AI-forward fusion vendors publish named government reference wins or independent (not vendor-asserted) time-to-resolution benchmarks, the claim graduates from positioning to a defensible differentiator; if not, it stays a watchlist item.
  1. Openness and integration versus walled garden. Whether a vendor is source-agnostic and integration-friendly — ingests rival vendors' collection, exposes APIs, lets the buyer keep multiple collection vendors — or steers the buyer into a single-vendor full stack. The public proxy is explicit "bring your own source" and open-connector positioning (Siren, DataWalk, i2) versus a collection vendor's fusion module that is most valuable atop that vendor's own collection. I read this as the axis most correlated with the grading line itself: the more open a fusion product, the more clearly it is a true standalone; the more it presumes the vendor's own collection, the closer it sits to being a collection-front dashboard that should be graded elsewhere.

The public barriers

The public barriers

Fusion's defining barrier is not technology — it is incumbency and switching cost, with no clean analog in the collection fronts. Two structural barriers sit on top of that.

Accreditation as the deployment gate. A fusion platform aggregates mixed-classification data inside accredited environments, so cloud and deployment accreditation is a hard gate for U.S.-federal and allied work. Palantir's posture is the clearest public evidence of the bar: it received "FedRAMP High Authorization" for its Federal Cloud Service covering "Palantir's entire product suite including Gotham" (announced December 2024), and holds Department of Defense "Impact Level 5 and 6" authorizations, later extended via "PFCS Forward" to on-premises and edge deployments.[^pg11] With an IL6 authorization, "government agencies can fast track use… in place of having to run lengthy and bespoke audit and accreditation processes" — which means an un-accredited fusion vendor faces a multi-quarter accreditation slog before it can even be deployed, a structural barrier that favors incumbents who already cleared it.[^pg11] One discipline binds throughout: any accreditation or certification claim about a named vendor must cite the certifying body or the vendor's own public statement, never an analyst assertion — a wrong accreditation claim on a named company is a credibility and a defamation hit.

Clearance and FOCI. For classified U.S. programs the vendor and its personnel must clear, and foreign ownership is a structural barrier independent of product quality. Contracts requiring access to classified information "will not be awarded to companies operating under foreign ownership, control or influence unless adequate safeguards are in place," administered through the Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency's SF 328 process, with mitigation instruments such as "independent directors, visitation and access controls, [and] restrictions on communications with the foreign owner."[^pg12] In May 2024 the Department of Defense expanded FOCI review "from solely US government contractors that access classified information to all US government contractors performing on certain unclassified contracts with a value exceeding US$5 million."[^pg13] For non-U.S. fusion vendors — Israeli, UK, or EU-headquartered — selling into U.S. classified programs, FOCI is the gate an excellent product cannot route around without a mitigation structure, which is itself a reason cross-border vendors in this market stand up or acquire cleared U.S. delivery entities. The specific magnitude and timeline of any individual vendor's FOCI posture is an inference from the regime's general applicability here, not a quoted fact — a thin spot worth naming as one.

Build-versus-buy and Palantir's incumbent gravity. This is the structural dynamic most specific to fusion, and it has two faces. The first is in-house build at the top tier: the largest national agencies have historically built their own analytics, and the public record on Palantir's early history notes that "the NSA in particular had been resistant because it had plenty of its own talent," reflecting "the agency's historical preference for developing solutions internally."[^pg14] In-house build is the structural "Tier 0" non-vendor competitor in fusion — at the very top end, the buyer's alternative to a commercial platform is its own engineering org, which caps commercial penetration of the most sensitive programs. The second face is switching cost once a platform is the system of record: Palantir's expansion, from "a single NSA contract" to "the data integration architecture for the Department of Defense, the Department of Homeland Security, the Internal Revenue Service" and beyond, with "$970.5 million" in U.S. federal contracts in 2025 — "nearly double the prior year," much of it via "a sequence of no-bid contracts" — is the public exemplar, and the switching cost is the ontology: the "framework that connects disparate data systems into a coherent operational model is what creates switching costs and defensibility."[^pg15][^pg16] I read Palantir's incumbency gravity as the dominant market-structure fact of this front: challengers (Siren, DataWalk, i2, Nuix) compete substantially on cost and openness against lock-in rather than on raw capability parity. If, through H2 2026, a federated or cost-positioned challenger publicly displaces an incumbent fusion deployment at a named agency — a named filing, procurement notice, or named-outlet report — the "incumbency is unassailable" read weakens; if challengers keep winning only greenfield and cost-sensitive deployments while incumbents hold the top-tier programs, the gravity read holds.

The government slice: a labeled proxy, not a segment size

No clean public dollar figure isolates the government and national-security data-fusion software slice, and given the opacity of B2G intelligence budgets, that is an expected, honest result, not a gap to be filled by invention. The three definitions above bracket adjacent markets; the government-fusion segment is the accreditation-gated subset that sits somewhere inside the crime-plus-defense-analytics envelope and well below the commercial DI total — but no publisher sells that number, and I do not manufacture one. The harder public currency for this front is not any market derivative at all. It is the single named public issuer in it.

That issuer is Palantir, the only pure-play public company in the front, which makes its disclosed Government-segment revenue the one hard, filing-grade datapoint here — carried strictly as the disclosed segment, never as a market size and never as total company revenue. For the three months ended 31 March 2026 (Q1 FY2026), Palantir reported Government-segment revenue of USD 858M, which it stated grew 76% year-over-year — with U.S. Government revenue at USD 687M (+84%) and International Government at USD 172M (+51%); total company revenue was USD 1.63B (+85%), carried here only as disclosed company context, not as an F4 market figure.[^t9] I read a single vendor's disclosed government revenue running at roughly a USD 3.4B annualized rate (four times the disclosed ~USD 858M quarter — stated as arithmetic on the published figure, not a publisher number) and growing 76% year-over-year as the cleanest public evidence that government fusion and operational-analytics demand is real and accelerating, even though no publisher isolates the segment. If Palantir's Government-segment year-over-year growth decelerates below ~20% across two consecutive quarters through 2026, the "accelerating government fusion demand" read weakens.

Demand drivers

Demand drivers

Three forcing functions carry the analytical weight the market numbers cannot.

The data has outrun the analyst. The same gap the collection fronts name from the supply side reappears here from the demand side: the volume and heterogeneity of available data — signals, OSINT, records, geospatial, biometric, financial — has outrun any analyst team's ability to correlate it manually. The defense-analytics publishers frame the driver as "multi-source data acquisition," "integrated data-management platforms," and "real-time operational intelligence";[^t6] the crime-analytics publishers frame it as the need to link and resolve patterns across "diverse data sources."[^t4] Fusion is the layer that monetizes that gap directly, and Cognyte states the need in its own public terms — NEXYTE "Preloads OSINT, BIZINT and commercial data into one intelligence view" and is built to "Fuse and analyze data across sources to resolve identities and uncover relations across datasets."[^t11] The structural point: every new collection source — a new OSINT feed, a new intercept modality, a new biometric database — increases the value of the fusion layer that correlates it. The analytical layer's market is a derivative of the collection fronts' growth.

Cross-agency fusion centers are institutionalizing shared analytics. The fusion-center model — a dedicated hub where multi-source data is collected, integrated, analyzed, and disseminated across agencies — is a named, standing institutional buyer of fusion software, distinct from any single agency's own stack. The U.S. operates a network of state, local, and federal fusion centers, and the demand is framed explicitly around eliminating data silos for cross-agency collaboration, with one fusion-center platform vendor publicly claiming adoption by "over 30% of U.S. fusion centers."[^t12][^t13] I read the fusion-center channel as the structural reason this front has a cross-agency, not just per-agency, buyer — the analytical layer is bought specifically to span organizational silos. If 2026–2027 fusion-center procurements consolidate onto in-house or single-prime stacks rather than commercial platforms, the cross-agency commercial-fusion read weakens.

Build-versus-buy is contested at the top tier. The sharpest 2025 public signal on demand structure is the build-versus-buy fight at top-tier agencies, crystallized in Palantir's GAO protest against the Defense Intelligence Agency. On 30 June 2025 Palantir filed a protest challenging the DIA's plan to award a sole-source contract for custom in-house software development — the "Prometheus" program, which began as a small-business research project — arguing the approach "violates the Competition in Contracting Act and Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act," which prefer commercial products; the DIA countered that "it does not have the software rights or documentation that the agency can share with a group of competitors."[^t14] I read the Prometheus protest as the clearest public expression of the front's demand fault line — commercial-off-the-shelf fusion platforms versus agency-built custom systems — and as confirmation that the boundary is contestable and recompeting rather than settled, which is exactly what creates the opening commercial vendors press into. If GAO or the courts side with the DIA's sole-source custom-build rationale, or the pattern repeats at other agencies through 2026, the "commercial COTS displaces in-house build" thesis weakens; if Palantir's protest succeeds — echoing its 2016 Army DCGS win at the Court of Federal Claims — it strengthens.

The AI and LLM shift

The AI and LLM shift

The clearest 2025–2026 public tech signal in fusion is that agentic AI on operational data has become the category benchmark, and Palantir's AIP is the most-cited public reference point — framed strictly as what vendors publicly claim, because true architecture cannot be verified from outside.

Palantir's own public positioning leads: AIP "connects AI with your data and operations" and is "designed to drive automation across operational processes," letting organizations "embed large language models (LLMs) and AI agents directly into the systems your teams use every day, enabling them to automate decisions, analyze enormous datasets, and execute tasks," with builder tools that "enable the development of production-ready AI-powered workflows, agents, and functions."[^t15] Palantir also states publicly that AIP "provides tools for generating detailed audit trails, explanations, and evaluations of model decisions"[^t15] — the governance framing that matters most for a defense and B2G buyer. The benchmark effect ripples across the rest of the front: Cognyte markets NEXYTE's "agentic AI-based decision flows" and integrated NEXYTE with Microsoft Azure to enhance its AI data-analytics solution,[^t11][^t16] and i2 Group brought "i2 Insights for assisted data importing and analysis" to market in its FY2025.[^t17]

The shared public claim across these vendors is that large-language-model and agentic AI compress the analyst workflow from raw multi-source data to decision-ready output. You cannot verify the true architecture from outside, so the discipline is to treat this as "markets itself as agentic," not "is agentic." The observable proxies are public product-page repositioning toward "agentic," named government accreditations covering the AI product (Palantir's AIP falls under its FedRAMP High authorization, as the barriers section noted), and named government reference wins. If a fusion vendor's AI positioning fails to convert into a named, dated government reference win or accreditation through 2026–2027, this trend demotes from a load-bearing convergence pillar to a watchlist item; for now, several of these claimants can be named from public pages, which is what keeps it a live candidate.

Procurement and accreditation ...

Procurement and accreditation structure

Fusion procurement is bimodal. At the very top tier it tracks the sovereign, sole-source pattern of the collection fronts — custom build versus prime award — while the broader law-enforcement and defense-analytics layer is bought through the same accreditation-gated federal vehicles as the rest of government software. Both modes are public-anchorable.

The gating barrier is federal cloud accreditation, and Palantir's public history is the cleanest table-stakes anchor: it holds DoD IL5 and IL6 accreditation for its Federal Cloud Service, with the IL6 Provisional Authorization announced 10 October 2022, and was granted FedRAMP High Baseline Authorization on 3 December 2024, stated to cover "the entirety of Palantir's product offerings — including AIP, Apollo, Foundry, Gotham, FedStart, and Mission Manager."[^t18][^t19] Palantir even markets the accreditation itself as a product through "FedStart," which lets third-party software run inside its "already-accredited environment."[^t20] I read the accreditation stack — IL5/IL6 plus FedRAMP High covering the AI product — as the real barrier to entry in the U.S. government fusion market: a software-quality-agnostic gate that favors incumbents who have already paid the accreditation cost. If a new fusion entrant wins a named U.S. classified-workload contract in 2026 without IL5-or-higher accreditation, the accreditation-as-moat read is over-stated.

Above that floor sits the sole-source-versus-recompete fault line. 4.6 Winning & Losing

4.6 Winning & Losing

Three patterns govern who is gaining ground and who is ceding it on the fusion / decision-intelligence front in 2026. Each is anchored to a specific public event, framed explicitly as opinion, and stated as a falsifiable prediction the next two quarters will confirm or refute. Read them in sequence: the first is the gravitational fact of the front — one vendor is hardening from supplier into infrastructure, and there is a public test of whether that grip holds. The second is the consolidation move every challenger is making in response — buying or federating the graph layer to assemble a complete stack. The third is the unresolved contest underneath both — whether the decades-old link-analysis standard gets displaced by the AI-native platforms, or modernizes and keeps its base. The scorecard on that last one is genuinely mixed, and the chapter says so.

### Pattern Claim 1 — The Palantir-Gravity Thesis

>

Observation. In March 2026 the U.S. Deputy Secretary of Defense directed that Palantir's Maven Smart System be designated a Pentagon "program of record" across all military branches, moving oversight to the DoD Chief Digital and AI Office and the U.S. Army for future contracting — converting a sequence of pilots into stable, multi-year core infrastructure.[^wl1] The scale underneath the designation is filing-grade: Palantir reported Government-segment revenue of $858M for the quarter ended 31 March 2026, up 76% year-over-year (U.S. Government $687M, +84%), against a >$4B-cash, zero-debt balance sheet.[^wl2] [^wl3] The deployment moat is publicly observable: Palantir holds FedRAMP High authorization stated to cover "the entirety of Palantir's product offerings — including AIP, Apollo, Foundry, Gotham, FedStart, and Mission Manager," plus DoD Impact Level 5 and Impact Level 6 authorizations.[^wl4] [^wl5] Independent analysis of the platform frames the switching cost as the ontology — the "framework that connects disparate data systems into a coherent operational model is what creates switching costs and defensibility."[^wl6]

>

My read. I read this as one vendor hardening from "vendor" into "infrastructure" inside the U.S. defense estate, on three reinforcing public legs: a program-of-record designation that locks in multi-year funding, a Government-segment revenue line that dwarfs every specialist in the front, and an accreditation ledger (FedRAMP High + IL5/IL6 covering the AI product) that is itself a software-quality-agnostic barrier to entry. The challengers in this front — i2, Nuix, Siren — compete substantially on cost and openness-against-lock-in rather than on raw capability parity, because the gravity is structural, not merely competitive. The single most important public test of whether that grip is total runs through the build-vs-buy fault line: the Palantir–DIA "Prometheus" GAO protest (filed 30 June 2025), in which Palantir challenged the Defense Intelligence Agency's plan to award a sole-source contract for custom in-house software development, arguing the approach "violates the Competition in Contracting Act and Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act," while DIA countered that "it does not have the software rights or documentation that the agency can share with a group of competitors."[^wl7] The in-house build remains the genuine Tier-0 competitor at the very top tier, and the boundary is contestable, not settled.

>

Conditional prediction. If H2 2026 brings a second allied government designating a Palantir platform as a standing program of record (not a pilot), or the DIA "Prometheus" custom-build path is forced back into a commercial recompete on the public record, the gravity thesis hardens into the defining structure of the front. If instead public procurement records show a named agency dual-sourcing fusion — running i2 or a federated alternative alongside Palantir on the same mission — or GAO/courts sustain DIA's sole-source custom-build rationale and the in-house-build pattern repeats at other top-tier agencies, the lock-in is less complete than the program-of-record framing implies, and the build option is reasserting itself at the ceiling Palantir cannot price into.

### Pattern Claim 2 — The Layer-Consolidation Thesis

>

Observation. Across 2025–2026 the fusion front's challengers moved to complete the stack by acquiring or federating the graph / entity-resolution layer rather than building it organically. Nuix — a processing-first vendor whose engine ingests "over 1000 file types" of unstructured data — signed to acquire Paris-based graph-intelligence specialist Linkurious for up to €20M (signed 3 December 2025, completion April 2026 following French FDI approval), folding link-analysis directly into its platform.[^wl8] [^wl9] On the architectural flank, Siren markets the federated alternative — its Federate technology "enhances Elasticsearch with complex join operations and powerful data-linking capabilities, allowing seamless data correlation across multiple indices" without centralizing the data[^wl10] — and assembled its entity-resolution capability through a Senzing partnership (announced 15 October 2024) and a strategic investment from Elastic (announced 2 December 2025), the latter "to advance AI-driven investigations for national security."[^wl11] [^wl12]

>

My read. I read this as two routes to the same destination — a complete collect-process-link-decide workflow — chosen by vendors that lead on one layer and lack another. Nuix takes the inorganic route: buy the graph layer outright and bolt it onto a best-in-class processing engine. Siren takes the architectural route: federate over data where it lives and partner in entity resolution, betting specifically on the classification- and sovereignty-constrained government buyer for whom bulk-copying mixed-classification data into one vendor's lake is itself a security and policy problem, not merely a cost question. Both are competing against Palantir's built ontology and i2's organic incumbency by assembling the missing layer rather than out-engineering the whole stack. The capital signal of 2025–2026 confirms the pattern: the fusion front's money is going into consolidation at the analytics layer — Nuix folding in Linkurious, Siren taking a strategic check from Elastic — not into greenfield standalone bets, which are scarce.

>

Conditional prediction. If H2 2026 brings a second comparable acquisition of a graph / link-analysis or entity-resolution specialist by a fusion-adjacent vendor, or a named government agency publicly adopts a federated-fusion architecture for a mission previously run on a central-lake platform — explicitly citing data-residency / no-bulk-copy as the selection rationale — then layer-consolidation is confirmed as the front's defining consolidation mode and the federated wedge graduates from positioning into a procurement-grade discriminator. If instead the acquired graph capabilities show up only as bolt-on features without named government reference wins, and federated deployments stay confined to data-residency-constrained niches without a named central-lake displacement, then the build (Palantir) and incumbency (i2) routes are out-competing the assemble-the-layer route, and consolidation is a financing story rather than a competitive realignment.

### Pattern Claim 3 — The Incumbent-Standard Stress Test

>

Observation. The front's central tension is whether the AI-native operationalization platforms displace the decades-old link-analysis standard, or whether the incumbent standard modernizes and retains its base — and the public scorecard is mixed, with both the disruptor and the incumbent growing double digits. On the disruptor side, Palantir positions AIP as the layer that lets organizations "embed large language models (LLMs) and AI agents directly into the systems your teams use every day,"[^wl13] and Nuix Neo's ACV surged 148% year-over-year in its first half of FY2026 as the platform reached roughly 20% of total ACV.[^wl14] On the incumbent side, i2 Group — the entity-link-property analyst workbench that a vast installed base across "more than 140 countries," including most members of NATO, trained on[^wl15] — disclosed "a 40% year-on-year increase in bookings and continued double-digit organic growth across all business segments" (7 October 2025), and brought "i2 Insights for assisted data importing and analysis" to market as its AI-enabled modernization layer over the established graph.[^wl15] [^wl16] i2 sits inside Constellation Software via N. Harris Computer (acquired from IBM in 2022), which does not break out the i2 line, so its segment financials are recorded as undisclosed.[^wl17]

>

My read. I read this as displacement-at-the-margin, not wholesale replacement — yet — and that hedge is the finding, not a failure to commit. Both halves of the contest are posting double-digit growth from public disclosures: Palantir/Nuix on the AI-native operationalization story, i2 on incumbency plus an AI-enabled modernization of the same graph core it has shipped for decades. The legacy moat here is real and quantifiable — a NATO-wide installed base trained on one methodology is a switching cost no homepage repositioning erases in a quarter — which is why I score this MIXED rather than calling a winner. The genuine question is not who markets AI on the graph (they all do) but whether the AI-native platforms can produce named government deployments that explicitly replace a legacy link-analysis system, as opposed to winning greenfield missions alongside the incumbent's retained base.

>

Conditional prediction. If through H2 2026 i2's public bookings growth decelerates while Palantir or Nuix publish named government reference wins that explicitly replace a legacy link-analysis deployment, the displacement thesis hardens and the incumbent standard is being eroded at the core, not just the margin. If i2 keeps posting double-digit bookings growth and publishes its own AI-native modernization with named retention wins — or the parent begins disclosing the line as a growth segment — then the "incumbent standard modernizes and holds" read wins, and the AI-native platforms are taking the greenfield while the installed base stays put.
