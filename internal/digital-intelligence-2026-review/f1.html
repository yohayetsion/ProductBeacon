Section 2

Front-matter glossary.

  • SIGINT (signals intelligence) — intelligence derived from intercepting and analysing signals, of which communications intelligence (COMINT) is the network-carried sub-discipline; F1's strategic variant is standing, mass-scale, network-side collection at national-backbone scale, the opposite of a deployed field team's portable collection.
  • DPI (deep packet inspection) — technology that classifies network traffic by application and protocol "up to layer 7 and beyond" and extracts metadata and, where decryptable, content, at line rate; a technology used in both F1 (mass/strategic) and F2 (warrant-scoped), not a front in itself.1
  • CDR / IPDR (call-detail record / IP-detail record) — structured metadata records of communications events — who contacted whom, when, for how long, over which application or endpoint — the durable, encryption-resilient yield of backbone collection, as distinct from intercepted content.
  • Mass profiling — population-scale construction of behavioural and relational profiles over collected network data; named in this chapter as public vendor positioning, never asserted as a verified capability.
  • Relation analytics — analytics that map relationships across collected communications data to "expose networks, surface relevant entities and generate investigative leads," turning a population of records into a graph.2
  • Crypto de-anonymization — resolution of identities, or de-anonymization of traffic, at population scale; named in this chapter strictly as public vendor positioning where it appears, never asserted, and read against the structural encryption headwind that cuts against it.
  • National-backbone collection — collection performed by a standing, passive probe wired into a carrier core, an international gateway, or an Internet Exchange Point, sized for terabit throughput and petabyte retention — the F1 defining property of volume plus standing infrastructure.
  • Encrypted-traffic intelligence (ETI) — the vendor category for machine-learning classification of encrypted flows by behaviour and statistical fingerprint, marketed as restoring visibility that TLS 1.3, ECH, and QUIC remove from classic cleartext inspection.1

1.1 The Playing Ground

1.1 The Playing Ground

National-agency SIGINT is the top of the collection stack. It is the front where an agency collects and analyses communications at the scale of a country's network backbone, standing, continuously, and remotely. Every front below this one collects against a narrower aperture. In Front 2, a law-enforcement unit serves a warrant on a Monitoring Center for a named target. In Front 3, a deployed team stands up an RF sensor near a present target in the field. This front is different in one defining way: the sensor is wired into the infrastructure itself. The buyer is not a police unit but a National Security, National Intelligence, or Military Intelligence agency. It needs to see, profile, and relation-map a population of communications as infrastructure — plumbed into the carrier core or the international gateway, sized in terabits per second and petabytes of retention, running without pause. The vendors name the property themselves. Vehere markets its national-scale offering as "a passive, packet-based interception system that empowers defense and intelligence agencies to detect and disrupt terror and criminal activities," one that "silently captures and analyzes network packets without altering or interfering with communication channels ensuring non-intrusive data collection," delivering "Terabit-speed packet capture with petabyte-scale storage and retrieval in seconds," deployable "at international gateways, Internet Exchange Points (IXPs), or within state-level and national networks."3 "Terabit-speed," "petabyte-scale," "national networks" — the vendor's own lexicon names the single property that fixes the chapter: volume plus standing infrastructure. Tactical collection is portable and present-tense; strategic collection is fixed and continuous.

The category is anchored just as cleanly from the agency-build side. The public record of how a top-tier agency does this when it builds rather than buys describes the same architecture: NSA "upstream collection" is publicly defined as "intercepting telephone and Internet traffic from the Internet backbone, meaning major Internet cables and switches," feeding a "processing and query system" — XKEYSCORE — supplied by "a constant flow of Internet traffic from fiber optic cables that make up the backbone of the world's communication network."45 "The backbone," "national networks," "at scale": the public lexicon, vendor and agency alike, names the defining property. So the boundary that keeps this chapter coherent has to be stated with unusual precision, because the technology rhymes across fronts even as the legal posture and the scale do not:

Front 1 is strategic, standing, mass-scale passive collection and analytics over network and signals data at national-backbone scale — defined by volume plus standing infrastructure, not by any single technology it touches. The bright line against Front 2 is the legal trigger plus the scale, not the technology: DPI lives in both fronts. DPI is a technology, not a front.

That sentence does the central grading work of the chapter, so it is worth unpacking what it includes and excludes.

What this front IS. Strategic, standing, mass-scale passive collection plus analytics over network and signals data, sized to a national backbone. The recurring capabilities across the public vendor pages and the public agency record are:

  • bulk metadata collection (CDR / IPDR);
  • deep-packet inspection at carrier scale to classify protocols and applications and extract metadata and, where decryptable, content;
  • mass profiling and relation analytics over the collected population;
  • application and protocol resolution at layer 7;
  • and — as publicly positioned by some vendors, never asserted here as fact — de-anonymization and identity resolution at scale.

The form factor is the tell: a passive probe wired into a carrier core, an international gateway, or an IXP, sized for terabit throughput and petabyte retention, running continuously. That is the opposite of Front 3's backpack.

What this front is NOT. Three boundaries matter, and the first is the one this chapter is built around.

First, it is not warrant-scoped, per-target lawful interception delivered to a LEA Monitoring Center — that is Front 2. This is the boundary this front is built around, and it has to be stated precisely, because the technology overlaps and the front does not. An F2 Monitoring Center receives intercept product through the carrier, under a warrant, for a named target, mediated by the lawful-interception standards (ETSI TS 102 232; 3GPP TS 33.107 / 33.108; CALEA). F1 collects without a per-target warrant trigger, at population scale, standing. The bright line versus F2 is the legal trigger plus the scale — not the technology. Deep-packet inspection lives in both fronts: a DPI probe can serve a warrant-scoped F2 interception or a mass-strategic F1 collection. DPI is a technology, not a front. The same vendor — Vehere, ClearTrail, and others — can sell into both, and several explicitly do. The grade follows the deployment posture (mass and standing versus warrant-scoped and targeted), not the silicon. I read this legal-trigger-plus-scale line as the only defensible F1/F2 boundary, precisely because any technology test would split the same product across two fronts and double-count it. The front is defined by how, and under what authority, the box is deployed, which is observable in the vendors' own positioning — "national intelligence," "strategic," "mass" versus "lawful interception," "warrant," "handover."

Second, it is not field or proximity collection — that is Front 3. A tactical IMSI catcher or direction-finding kit collects locally, against a present target, portably. F1 collects remotely, at the backbone, standing. The catcher may feed identifiers into an F1 analytics platform, but the catcher is F3 and the standing backbone platform is F1.

Third, it is not the source-agnostic fusion and analytics layer sold standalone — that is Front 4. A mass-collection platform with a built-in profiling and relation-analytics dashboard is graded here, because its analytics are inseparable from its own collection at the carrier core. A source-agnostic fusion platform that ingests F1 output but performs no collection of its own is Front 4. Where a vendor sells both, the F1 grade rests only on the collection-and-analytics-at-scale product, and the standalone analytics platform is referenced to Front 4.

There is one more occupant of this front that no vendor list captures, and it sits above the commercial market rather than inside it.

The in-house build — the Tier-0 non-vendor force. At the very top of the buyer pyramid, the alternative to a commercial vendor is the agency's own engineering org. The public record on top-tier SIGINT shows exactly this: national agencies building national-scale collection and analytics themselves — NSA "upstream collection" tapping the Internet backbone, feeding the agency-built XKEYSCORE "processing and query system."45 I read the in-house build as the structural "Tier-0" non-vendor competitor in F1, discussable purely as a market-structure fact from the public record. At the top end — the largest, best-resourced national agencies — the buyer's alternative to a commercial DPI and collection platform is its own talent, which caps commercial-vendor penetration of the most sensitive national programs. The commercial F1 market is therefore structurally the second-tier-and-below of national buyers plus the export market — the agencies that cannot, or choose not to, build their own. This read is anchored only to the public NSA-era record; no internal or classified knowledge of any agency's build is used or implied.

Three buyer misconceptions

Three buyer misconceptions

"Mass collection means reading everyone's content." Increasingly, no — and this is the front's central technical story. The reliable, durable yield of backbone collection is metadata and traffic classification, not plaintext content, because transport encryption now defends most content end-to-end. The DPI vendors have visibly repositioned around this. ipoque (a Rohde & Schwarz company) markets its R&S PACE 2 engine to "identify and classify thousands of applications and protocols and extract metadata in real time, even if traffic is encrypted or obfuscated," via "encrypted traffic intelligence" that "combines machine learning, deep learning and high dimensional data analysis with advanced caching methods to identify and classify underlying traffic flows."1 I read the industry-wide pivot from "inspect content" to "classify-and-fingerprint encrypted flows" as the single most important technical fact in F1. The value proposition is migrating from content toward metadata, classification, and behavioural inference, because content is going dark — the same structural force that compresses Front 3's over-the-air interception, expressed at the network's scale. The buyer who thinks a backbone tap is a national wiretap is buying yesterday's network.

"DPI tells you who everyone is talking to." Less and less, at the front edge of the protocol stack. The last cleartext field that DPI traditionally keyed on — the TLS Server Name Indication (SNI) — is being encrypted by Encrypted Client Hello (ECH), and QUIC moves most of the handshake into encrypted, UDP-borne packets. Per public technical analysis, ECH "wraps the standard Server Name Indication (SNI) within an encrypted key exchange," so "SNI filtering... breaks when ECH is in play," and one industry source reports that "59% of browsers support Encrypted Client Hello (ECH), hiding destination information and eliminating the last piece of cleartext visibility in TLS/QUIC handshake."67 I read ECH and QUIC as the structural headwind on classic passive content-and-SNI inspection — which is exactly why every serious DPI vendor now markets ML-based "encrypted traffic intelligence," fingerprinting flows by behaviour because the cleartext labels are disappearing. (The ~59% ECH figure is a single-source datapoint and is carried as directional, to be re-verified rather than relied upon.)

"Crypto de-anonymization at national scale is a solved, off-the-shelf capability." It is vendor positioning where it appears at all, and is treated as such throughout this chapter. Some vendors market identity-resolution and de-anonymization at scale; this report quotes such claims only as that vendor's public positioning, attributed, and never asserts that any vendor "can" de-anonymize at national scale. The capability is, by its nature, not externally verifiable. The strongest crypto headwind cuts directly against it: transport encryption with Perfect Forward Secrecy, plus 5G's encryption of the permanent subscriber identifier (SUPI is concealed and the encrypted SUCI transmitted). I read crypto-de-anon-at-scale as the F1 claim with the widest gap between marketing and outside-verifiability, and the one most exposed to the encryption headwind. The honest public posture is that it is an aspiration vendors position around, not a verified national capability. A named independent benchmark or reference win is the only thing that would move it from claim to evidence, none of which is publicly available.

The data and technology stack

The data and technology stack

The F1 stack is best read as four layers the national buyer assembles, unified by the fact that the sensor is wired into the backbone, standing, and sized for volume.

The first and workhorse layer is bulk access and capture at the carrier core, gateway, or IXP — terabit-speed, petabyte-scale. The workhorse is a passive probe at a high-volume aggregation point. Vehere markets "High speed and real-time passive interception platforms for lossless data extractions with next-gen DPI probe" and "Terabit-speed packet capture with petabyte-scale storage and retrieval in seconds," deployable "at international gateways, Internet Exchange Points (IXPs), or within state-level and national networks."3 The public NSA record describes the same architecture at the agency-build extreme — "upstream" collection tapping the physical infrastructure the Internet uses to route traffic, with named programs feeding XKEYSCORE.45 I read "passive, at the gateway or IXP" as the architectural signature of F1. Passivity — no transmission, non-intrusive — plus a backbone aggregation point is what delivers volume plus standing, the property the front is defined by. And it is publicly observable in the vendors' own deployment-location language.

The second layer is DPI and metadata extraction — classify, then extract. DPI is the engine that turns raw backbone packets into structured intelligence. It classifies which application or protocol a flow is, and extracts metadata and, where available, content. ipoque markets R&S PACE 2 as "an advanced protocol and application classification engine based on next-gen deep packet inspection (DPI) technology" delivering "real-time IP network traffic visibility up to layer 7 and beyond."1 Allot markets its network-intelligence line as the ability to "Generate actionable intelligence through analysis of every packet of network, user, application, and security data," delivering "carrier grade solutions that meet government security requirements" with "the capacity to manage up to 1Tbps of traffic."8 I read DPI as the genuinely hard, capital-intensive table-stakes of F1, because sustaining "every packet" classification at carrier line rate (the 1Tbps figure) is an engineering moat. The public proxy for it is throughput claims plus classified-application count, both of which vendors publish, rather than de-anonymization, which they do not substantiate. (Allot's scale figures are vendor self-reported and directional, not independently verified.)

The third layer is encrypted-traffic intelligence — the machine-learning pivot as cleartext disappears. Because transport encryption is removing the cleartext fields DPI traditionally read, the front has pivoted to ML classification of encrypted flows. ipoque markets R&S PACE 2 as "Future-proof: classifies encrypted (TLS 1.3, DoH, ESNI etc.) and obfuscated traffic," via ETI that "combines machine learning, deep learning and high dimensional data analysis... to identify and classify underlying traffic flows."1 The historical record on the same modality runs through PacketLogic, which AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) built on "a proprietary library of over 3,500 application signatures updated weekly," and the broader public technical literature describes "Encrypted Traffic Analytics... analyzing unique patterns in just the TLS Client Hello packet to derive actionable intelligence without requiring full decryption."97 I read encrypted-traffic intelligence as the defining technical contest of F1 in 2026: the winners are the engines that hold classification accuracy as the cleartext labels (SNI, handshake) vanish. "Classifies encrypted traffic" is therefore a positioning claim about ML fingerprinting accuracy, not a verifiable statement that content is being read. The credible public proxy is the published detection-rate claim plus the named protocol set (TLS 1.3 / QUIC / DoH), not any content-interception assertion.

The fourth layer is mass profiling and relation analytics over the collected population. Once collected, the population of metadata and classified flows is profiled and relation-mapped. ClearTrail markets solutions that "enable law enforcement agencies (LEAs) to perform MASS, TARGETED & TACTICAL Monitoring and Analysis across a variety of communication networks," transforming "communication data into actionable intelligence" via "data integration and advanced data analytics by leveraging artificial intelligence models on big data."10 I read the analytics layer as the point where F1 most blurs into Front 4, because mass profiling over collected data is analytics. The grading discipline resolves it: where the analytics are inseparable from the vendor's own backbone collection, they are F1; where the vendor sells the analytics layer standalone to bring-your-own-source buyers, that product is Front 4. The same vendor can land in both fronts, on two different products. (ClearTrail's positioning explicitly spans the mass and the targeted, so its F1 grade must rest on the mass-and-strategic pillar; the same line is the warrant-scoped business that belongs to F2.)

The axes buyers discriminate on

Six dimensions separate the serious national-scale vendors, each mapping to a real, publicly observable cost-and-capability structure — with the crypto-de-anon and mass-profiling dimensions treated strictly as positioning.

  1. DPI depth and protocol coverage. How many applications and protocols a system classifies, and how deep — layer 7 "and beyond." The public proxy is the published classified-application count and the signature-update cadence: ipoque's "thousands of applications and protocols," "highest traffic detection rate on the market," weekly updates; the historical PacketLogic "over 3,500 application signatures updated weekly."19 Every serious vendor now claims thousands of signatures plus weekly updates. I read raw signature count as necessary table-stakes, not the discriminator. The real, harder-to-verify differentiator has moved to accuracy on encrypted and obfuscated traffic, where the cleartext signature is gone. If, through H2 2026, a DPI vendor publishes an independent benchmark of classification accuracy on TLS 1.3 plus QUIC plus ECH traffic — not a self-asserted one — the "signature count is table-stakes, encrypted-accuracy is the real axis" read hardens, while with no independent benchmarks all "classifies encrypted traffic" claims stay vendor positioning.
  1. Throughput and mass-scale — the axis that is the front. Sustained classification and capture at carrier line rate. This is the cleanest public discriminator in F1, because vendors publish it: Vehere's "terabit-speed... petabyte-scale," Allot's "up to 1Tbps of traffic" and "analysis of every packet."38 I read throughput as the F1 analog of Front 3's form factor — the visible, hard-to-fake discriminator — because sustaining "every packet" at terabit rates is a capital and engineering reality, not a slide. If vendors continue to lead with throughput and retention figures (Tbps, PB) in their public national-grade positioning through H2 2026, the "throughput is the public discriminator" read holds. It weakens only if the front's public marketing pivots wholly to AI and analytics and drops the scale numbers — which would itself signal the content-value erosion the next axis names.
  1. Encrypted-traffic erosion (TLS 1.3 / ECH / QUIC) — the structural headwind. The defining macro-force on F1, exactly parallel to Front 3's encryption compressing classic passive interception, and it works through three compounding effects. First, TLS 1.3 with Perfect Forward Secrecy makes passive out-of-band decryption impossible — per public technical analysis, "TLS 1.3 mandates Perfect Forward Secrecy, so passive out-of-band decryption is no longer possible."7 Second, ECH encrypts the last cleartext label, "eliminating the last piece of cleartext visibility in TLS/QUIC handshake."76 Third, QUIC moves the handshake into encrypted UDP packets, breaking classic TCP-oriented DPI. The classic passive content-and-SNI inspection value proposition is structurally shrinking as encryption hardens. I read this as the force pushing the entire front from content toward metadata, ML-based flow fingerprinting, and behavioural inference — which is why every DPI vendor now markets "encrypted traffic intelligence" rather than "content interception." If, through H2 2026 and into 2027, vendor roadmaps and named-outlet coverage keep foregrounding ML encrypted-traffic classification over classic content interception, the compression read holds. It weakens if a vendor publicly demonstrates durable, independently verified content visibility into TLS 1.3 plus ECH plus QUIC at backbone scale.
  1. Mass-profiling capability — positioning, not asserted capability. How well a system builds population-scale profiles and relation graphs over collected data. This is framed as how a vendor markets itself, not as a fact. The public proxy is explicit profiling and relation-analytics positioning (ClearTrail's "MASS... Monitoring and Analysis").10 I read mass-profiling claims as the analytics-layer positioning that most needs the grading discipline applied. A "mass profiling" claim inseparable from the vendor's own backbone collection is F1 positioning; the same capability sold standalone is Front 4. The accuracy and scale are not outside-verifiable, so it is reported as positioning with the named-reference proxy as the only escalation path.
  1. Crypto de-anonymization at scale — positioning only, handled with maximum strictness. Whether a system can resolve identities or de-anonymize traffic at population scale. This chapter makes no assertion that any vendor can do this. Such a claim enters only if a vendor states it publicly, quoted and attributed as that vendor's positioning. The strongest structural countercurrent is documented: 5G encrypts the permanent subscriber identifier and transmits the encrypted SUCI, and transport encryption with PFS removes passive decryption — both cut against de-anon at scale. I read crypto-de-anon-at-scale as the single F1 claim with the widest marketing-to-verifiability gap. The honest public posture is that it is an aspiration vendors position around, citing the encryption headwind as the structural reason for skepticism, without naming any vendor as capable.
  1. Deployment resilience. Whether a system sustains collection through network change — new protocols, encryption shifts, the 2G/3G sunset, carrier-topology change — without re-engineering. The public proxy is "future-proof" positioning, signature-update cadence, and ML-adaptation claims (ipoque's "Future-proof... classifies encrypted (TLS 1.3, DoH, ESNI)," weekly updates).1 I read resilience as a roadmap-velocity axis — the front's macro-headwind means the durable winner is whoever adapts classification fastest as encryption and protocols move, which is why "future-proof, weekly-updated, ML-based" is the recurring public claim. The verifiable proxy is update cadence and the named-protocol set the vendor commits to publicly, not the unfalsifiable "future-proof" label itself.

The public barriers

The public barriers

This front's defining barrier is not technology — it is export control, which is also the legitimacy frame for the entire national-surveillance discussion. And naming the right control line matters, because F1's modality — IP-network surveillance — sits under a different control category from the mobile-implant and intrusion-software class that Front 3 carves out, and conflating them blurs the boundary.

Export-control regimes — the defining public barrier. National-scale network-surveillance equipment is dual-use, and three overlapping regimes gate who may sell it across borders. The Wassenaar Arrangement controls "Internet Protocol (IP) network communications surveillance systems or equipment, and test, inspection, production equipment, specially designed components therefor, and development and production software and technology therefor."1112 The F1-relevant control is the IP-network-surveillance line under Category 5 Part 1 (Telecommunications) — distinct from the intrusion-software line under Category 4 (Computers) that governs the mobile-implant class carved out of Front 3.11 I read the Category 5 Part 1 placement as the cleanest official articulation of the F1 boundary itself. F1's mass-network-surveillance equipment is a Telecommunications-category control, where F3's carved-out implant class is a Computers-category intrusion-software control. The cross-front boundary is encoded in the export-control taxonomy, not asserted by this report.

The US controls track the same line and are unusually precise about what makes a system an F1-grade national-surveillance box. EAR ECCN 5A001.j captures "IP network communications surveillance systems or equipment, and 'specially designed' components therefor" meeting a list of specifications "on a carrier class IP network (e.g., national grade IP backbone)" — including "[a]nalysis at the application layer," extraction of "selected metadata and application content (e.g., voice, video, messages, attachments)," "indexing of extracted data," execution of "searches on the basis of 'hard selectors,'" and mapping "the relational network of an individual or of a group of people."1314 The 5A001.j definition is, in effect, a regulator's specification of an F1 system: "carrier class... national grade IP backbone," application-layer analysis, metadata-plus-content extraction, hard-selector search, relational-network mapping. I read this as the single best public table-stakes description of the front. The capability list this chapter opened with (DPI, mass profiling, relation analytics, application resolution) is the same list the US export regime uses to define the controlled item. So the front's existence and shape are anchorable to a primary legal instrument, not to internal knowledge.

The EU layer adds a human-rights end-use test. Under Dual-Use Regulation 2021/821, Article 5 imposes an authorization requirement on the export of non-listed cyber-surveillance items where "the exporter has been informed by the relevant authority that the items in question are or may be intended for use in connection with internal repression and/or the commission of serious violations of human rights and international humanitarian law." It defines cyber-surveillance items as dual-use items "specially designed to enable the covert surveillance of natural persons by monitoring, extracting, collecting or analysing data from information and telecommunication systems." The Commission issued implementing guidelines on 15 October 2024.1516 I read the Article 5 catch-all as the regime most directly aimed at this front's reputational reality. It reaches IP-network and metadata-analysis surveillance items even when no specific listing applies, and imports a human-rights end-use test. So an EU-headquartered F1 vendor carries a due-diligence burden that is itself a competitive and reputational variable. Throughout, one discipline binds: any control-classification claim about a named vendor cites the regime text or the vendor's own statement, never an analyst assertion — a wrong classification on a named company is a credibility and a defamation hit.

The enforcement edge is real and named — the Sandvine case. The most concrete public proof that this barrier has teeth is the Sandvine / AppLogic episode, and it is also the front's cautionary public record. In February 2024 the US Department of Commerce added Sandvine Incorporated to the Entity List. Per the US Department of State, the action followed findings that Sandvine supplied deep-packet-inspection technology used by the Government of Egypt "in mass web-monitoring and censorship to block news as well as target political actors and human rights activists."1718 Separately, Citizen Lab's named research documented PacketLogic middleboxes being used for network injection — "Sandvine's PacketLogic Devices Used to Deploy Government Spyware" and, in the Eltantawy case, "a Sandvine PacketLogic middlebox" on "a link between Telecom Egypt and Vodafone Egypt" used to inject Predator spyware against a former Egyptian MP after he announced a presidential bid.1920 Commerce removed Sandvine from the Entity List in October 2024 "following significant corporate reforms," and the company rebranded as AppLogic Networks in March 2025.2122 (Every negative in this paragraph is attributed to a named outlet, an official record, or the company's own statement; this report asserts no negative about any named vendor on its own authority. Predator / Intellexa appears only as the named-outlet-documented injected payload, never as a graded contender.) I read the Sandvine episode as the front's defining public legitimacy fact. The same DPI-at-backbone capability that defines F1 is the capability regulators and researchers scrutinize for human-rights misuse. That is why export-control posture, end-use due diligence, and "we exited non-democratic markets / sell software only" positioning have become competitive and reputational variables in F1, not just compliance overhead.

Clearance and FOCI for US programs. For classified US national-security programs, the vendor and its personnel must clear, and foreign ownership is a structural barrier independent of product quality. That barrier is treated in depth in the Front 4 chapter (FOCI mitigation via the SF 328 process administered by DCSA) and applies to F1 vendors identically. For F1 specifically, FOCI compounds the in-house-build ceiling: the top-tier US buyer may build its own, and where it buys, the foreign-headquartered vendor faces a FOCI gate an excellent product cannot route around without a mitigation structure.

Market size: four named markets, walled, never averaged — and one honest non-claim

There is no clean public dollar figure for the F1 national mass-collection software slice. The honest way to size this front is to set the public estimates of the adjacent markets side by side and let the spread between definitions speak. The defining problem is structural. National mass-collection budgets are opaque or classified, much of the equipment is sold under export control, and the specialist vendors are mostly private. So no research publisher isolates the F1 mass-collection slice. What exists publicly are several adjacent markets — lawful interception, deep-packet inspection / network intelligence, signals intelligence, and telecom / network analytics. Each is scoped differently. Each is blended across F1 (mass strategic collection) and F2 (warrant-scoped lawful interception), or across the commercial technology layer. And each disagrees with the others by multiples. They are walled below and deliberately not summed — summing would multiply-count the vendors that appear in more than one definition. The spread between definitions, and the opacity within the F1 slice, is the finding. All figures are research-publisher numbers and are directional; none should be quoted as authoritative.

The first market is lawful interception, the broadest published figure that touches this terrain. But as published it blends warrant-scoped, carrier-mediated interception (Front 2's domain) with the network-collection plumbing that also serves strategic collection (F1). The publishers disagree wildly, on the level and on the trajectory alike. It is carried only as the outer interception envelope, with the explicit caveat that the F1 mass-collection slice is a small, opaque sub-fraction of it.

Demand drivers

Demand drivers

Four forcing functions carry the analytical weight the opaque market numbers cannot.

The encryption arms race compressing classic passive inspection — the structural ground. The root driver across F1 is that the spread of TLS 1.3, Encrypted Client Hello, QUIC / HTTP-3, and encrypted DNS is eroding classic passive network inspection. The bulk DPI that historically read application identity and metadata off the wire increasingly sees only ciphertext. The vendor framing is explicit. ipoque states that "Modern encryption protocols and standards increasingly restrict what data is exposed and analysis DPI tools can perform," and that "with newer protocols, metadata in the packets are often encrypted, making it harder to glean information from the encrypted payload or during protocol handshakes," with QUIC specifically hiding "the entire ClientHello" so "networks are no longer able to read the host name or the domain name of the website."40 The market response is encrypted-traffic intelligence. ipoque frames it as combining "improved DPI with artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) techniques to restore visibility into encrypted network traffic," and the academic record corroborates the difficulty — "TLS 1.3's security enhancements present unprecedented challenges for encrypted traffic analysis, rendering traditional methods designed for TLS 1.2 and earlier versions ineffective."4041 I read the encryption arms race as the single structural force reshaping F1. It compresses the value of classic passive payload inspection while expanding demand for metadata-, behavioural-, and ML-based profiling, the network-scale analogue of the wire-to-endpoint shift Front 3 sees. If DPI vendors' public messaging through 2026–2027 reverts to payload-inspection framing rather than ETI and metadata framing, the "encryption shifts demand to inference" read weakens. (Framed on vendor public positioning plus the published encryption-analysis literature; true collection architecture is not verifiable from outside.)

National cyber-sovereignty and data-localization mandates forcing in-country infrastructure. A measurable, dated forcing function is the wave of data-sovereignty and localization mandates that require interception and network infrastructure to sit on national territory — directly expanding national agencies' standing-collection footprint. The count is rising: "In 2017, 35 countries had localization or cross-border control requirements. By 2023, that figure had grown to 62."42 The interception-specific instance is concrete: in November 2025, the Netherlands, granting Starlink a license, "required that all lawful interception infrastructure sit on Dutch territory — explicitly to avoid data flowing through 'American servers beyond Dutch oversight,'" and India "now requires satellite operators to route data through domestic ground infrastructure and localize at least 20 percent of ground stations within five years."43 I read cyber-sovereignty as a demand multiplier specific to F1 — every localization mandate that forces interception or collection infrastructure in-country is a standing-infrastructure procurement, the exact volume-plus-standing-infrastructure property that defines F1. If the count of localization and in-country-interception mandates plateaus or reverses through 2026–2027, this read weakens.

The 5G standalone core build-out forcing a collection-architecture refresh. The migration to standalone 5G cores is a named, standards-driven refresh function. The lawful-interception architecture for 5G is fixed by ETSI / 3GPP standards: ETSI TS 133.127 (LI architecture) and TS 133.128 (LI delivery). These standards define new interception interfaces (X1 task management, X2 intercept-related information, X3 communication content) that operators migrating to 5G standalone must expose.44 Each generational transition (2G → 3G → 4G → 5G-SA) obsoletes a portion of the installed collection base and forces a standards-aligned refresh. I read the 5G-SA transition as a structural, standards-driven demand pulse that favours vendors who can track 3GPP releases. If 5G-SA-aligned collection and interception solutions fail to out-grow the legacy base through 2026–2027, the refresh-cycle read weakens. (Framed strictly on the public ETSI / 3GPP standards — no vendor-specific 5G-readiness claim is asserted, which would be a defamation and credibility surface.)

The build-versus-buy fault line at top-tier agencies — in-house "Tier-0." The structural non-vendor competitor in F1 is the top-tier agency that builds its own collection capability in-house rather than buying commercial. The largest SIGINT agencies publicly describe designing and developing their own collection mechanisms: the US National Security Agency states that it "designs and develops" SIGINT collection capability and consolidates collection within the agency.45 This sets a public "Tier-0" ceiling: the most capable buyers are also the ones most likely to build, which caps the commercial vendors' addressable top of market. I read in-house build as the market force that bounds F1's commercial TAM at the top. The highest-capability, highest-budget agencies (Five Eyes class) build their own and buy selectively, so the commercial F1 vendors' real addressable market skews toward the long tail of mid-tier national agencies that cannot sustain in-house capability. Public reporting of a top-tier agency materially shifting from in-house build to commercial procurement (or the reverse) through 2026–2027 would move this fault line. (Framed on the NSA's own public statements; no internal procurement knowledge.)

The tech trend: from payload to inference, with AI on collected data

The single clearest 2025–2026 public tech signal in F1 is the shift of the collection center-of-gravity from reading payload off the wire to inferring from metadata and behaviour, with AI and ML applied to the collected corpus. This is framed strictly as what the public record shows, not as verified architecture. As classic passive payload inspection is compressed by encryption, the public product motion is toward metadata and behavioural analysis (ML-based application and behaviour fingerprinting) and toward AI applied to whatever is collected. The vendor framing is explicit and quotable: ETI "combines improved DPI with artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) techniques to restore visibility into encrypted network traffic" and uses "behavioural, statistical and heuristic analyses" to "identify protocols, applications and services across encrypted traffic, in real time."40 You cannot verify the true collection architecture from outside. The observable proxies are public product-page repositioning toward ETI / AI / behavioural framing, the academic record confirming TLS-1.3 and QUIC analysis difficulty, and the health of the public DPI / network-intelligence issuer (Allot). I read the center of gravity as migrating from reading content off the wire to inferring from metadata and behaviour — which is why the DPI vendors' public messaging now centers on ETI and AI rather than payload inspection. If vendor positioning reverts to payload-decryption framing through 2026–2027, this demotes to a watchlist item rather than a load-bearing trend.

The procurement structure

The procurement structure

F1 procurement is the most opaque in the report at the buy side and the most export-control-and-sanctions-gated at the sell side: national mass-collection budgets are largely classified, so the procurement is rarely visible on public portals. The supply side, meanwhile, is gated by dual-use export licensing and increasingly reshaped by sanctions and accountability actions. The gates are public-anchorable even where the deals are not.

Export licensing as the gate. For national-scale network-surveillance equipment, export control is the single richest public table-stakes barrier — and doubles as the legitimacy frame for the surveillance-vendor discussion. The Wassenaar Arrangement controls IP-network surveillance systems under Category 5 Part 1 (Telecommunications) and intrusion software under Category 4 (Computers) — the multilateral basis the national regimes implement. EU Dual-Use Regulation 2021/821 implements the Wassenaar controls into the EU's binding dual-use list and adds the Article 5 catch-all for cyber-surveillance items not on the list where there is awareness of potential misuse. EU guidance defines controlled cyber-surveillance items to include "equipment for covertly extracting communication content and metadata that is transmitted over the air via wireless communication, and radiofrequency monitoring equipment," and the EU updated its dual-use control list on 8 September 2025.4647 The US implemented complementary EAR controls on cybersecurity items in March 2022, reaching IP-network communications surveillance (ECCN 5A001.j) and intrusion software (4D004).46 I read export licensing as the real barrier to entry in F1's network-collection segment: a regime-gated, licence-by-destination gate that constrains who can sell to whom independent of product quality. It is also the public lever through which accountability pressure reshapes the supply side. (Specific ECCN classifications are not asserted per vendor here; that is a defamation and credibility surface, and the regime, not a vendor-specific classification, is what is cited.)

The accountability and sanctions overhang reshaping who can sell — the Sandvine precedent. The most concrete, named, dated public signal that accountability pressure is reshaping the F1 supply side is the Sandvine case — a DPI / network-intelligence vendor sanctioned, materially impaired, and forced to restructure. On 27 February 2024 the US Bureau of Industry and Security added Sandvine to the Entity List, on information that Sandvine "supplies deep packet inspection technology to the Government of Egypt, where it is used in mass web-monitoring and censorship to block news as well as target political actors and human rights activists," with a license requirement for all EAR items and a "presumption of denial."48 The commercial impact was material and public: Sandvine announced in September 2024 it would withdraw from 56 "non-democratic" countries, which "represent approximately 45% of Sandvine's 2023 revenue."49 On 21 October 2024, BIS removed Sandvine from the Entity List "following significant corporate reforms to protect human rights," and the company subsequently completed a debt restructuring and emerged rebranded as AppLogic Networks.50 I read the Sandvine episode as the clearest public evidence that the sanctions and accountability overhang is now a first-order force on the F1 supply side. A single Entity-List designation cost a DPI vendor roughly 45% of its revenue base (its own disclosed figure) and forced a restructuring and rebrand. The mechanism is public, named, and dated; it concentrates the addressable supply toward vendors who can clear export licensing and survive scrutiny. A further named Entity-List or sanctions action materially constraining an F1 network-surveillance vendor through 2026 — named-outlet-attributed — would strengthen the read; such a claim is named-outlet-attributed or dropped. (All Sandvine claims here are stated facts of the public regulatory and press record — designation, stated rationale, the company's own disclosed revenue-exposure figure, removal, restructuring — not internal-knowledge characterizations.)

Sovereign procurement opacity. At the national mass-collection end, procurement is opaque sovereign buying, frequently classified, NDA-bound, or routed through defense-procurement channels that do not surface on public portals. A tender enters this report only via a public procurement portal or a named-outlet citation, dated — never from internal knowledge. Specific national-collection deal values are not publicly isolable and are carried as channel structure only, never as a sized figure. Layered on top of this opacity is a slow structural shift in the collection base itself. As of December 2025, "more than 300 2G and 3G switch-offs have been completed, planned, or are underway across 88 countries,"51 retiring the legacy networks that historically offered downgrade and legacy-collection paths. That is a quiet erosion of the collection surface that compounds the encryption headwind over time.

Play 1 — The Encrypted-Traffic-Erosion Pivot: from passive inspection to behavioral and metadata intelligence

Observation. The classic value proposition of mass passive collection — see the content, classify the application — is being structurally compressed by encryption, and the public record shows vendors pivoting from inspection to inference. The DPI-engine layer's public answer is not to decrypt but to fingerprint: ipoque (a Rohde & Schwarz company) markets its R&S®PACE 2 "Encrypted Traffic Intelligence," which "combines machine learning, deep learning and high dimensional data analysis" so the engine can "identify protocols, applications and services across encrypted traffic, in real time," and is "future-proof" enough to "classif[y] encrypted (TLS 1.3, DoH, ESNI etc.) and obfuscated traffic."77 AppLogic Networks (the rebranded Sandvine) publicly discusses combining identification techniques to maintain classification "even as encryption and transports evolve."78

Participants. ipoque / Rohde & Schwarz at the DPI-engine layer; AppLogic Networks on carrier-scale classification; and the network-intelligence vendors graded above — Vehere, ClearTrail, BAE Systems — whose mass-collection value depends on the engine layer keeping pace as encryption hardens.7778

That shift advantages the vendors who market ML- and DL-based encrypted-traffic classification, and the buyers who can build behavioral models at scale, and it erodes the value of decryption-dependent inspection. The same encryption wave that compresses the field-RF interception modality from one end of the network compresses national-backbone passive collection from the other.

1.6 Winning & Losing

1.6 Winning & Losing

Three pattern claims carry this front. Each opens with a dated public observation, separates that observation from my read of it, and ends with a conditional a publicly observable proxy can confirm or refute before the next refresh. They share one throughline, the structural fact the whole chapter is built on: transport encryption is hollowing out the classic value proposition of mass passive collection — read the content, classify the application. The value it sheds is migrating in two directions. It moves up the stack, from content toward metadata and behavioral inference. And it moves toward the accountable end of the supply side, where sanctions and export control — not capability alone — now gate who may sell mass-surveillance plumbing across borders. The three claims trace that migration from three angles. The first is where the technical value went (encrypted-traffic and behavioral intelligence). The second is who is allowed to sell at all (the accountability reset). The third is why the largest demand is structurally out of commercial reach in the first place (the sovereign-build ceiling).

A discipline note before the claims. This is the report's most clean-room-sensitive front. One rule governs every line below. The read is built only from dated public material — vendor pages, official records, named outlets, filings. The chapter never asserts a SIGINT capability; only vendor positioning is described. Where a vendor markets "mass profiling" or "crypto de-anonymization," that is reported as the vendor's public positioning, never as a verified capability; true collection reach cannot be read from outside, and this chapter does not pretend otherwise.

Pattern Claim 1 — The Encrypted-Traffic Erosion Pivot

Observation. The classic value proposition of mass passive collection — see the content, classify the application off the wire — is structurally compressing as encryption hardens, and the public record shows the vendors pivoting from inspection to inference. Roughly 94% of web traffic now runs over HTTPS; TLS 1.3 mandates Perfect Forward Secrecy, so "passive out-of-band decryption is no longer possible"; QUIC moves the handshake into encrypted UDP packets; and Encrypted Client Hello (ECH) encrypts the last cleartext label, the Server Name Indication, "eliminating the last piece of cleartext visibility in TLS/QUIC handshake."9596 (The ~94% HTTPS adoption figure is a directional, single-source industry-telemetry datapoint, carried for the encryption-erosion direction, not as a load-bearing number.) The DPI-engine layer's public answer is to stop trying to read content and start fingerprinting flows: ipoque (a Rohde & Schwarz company) markets "Encrypted traffic intelligence (ETI)" that "combines machine learning, deep learning and high dimensional data analysis … to identify and classify underlying traffic flows," classifying "encrypted (TLS 1.3, DoH, ESNI etc.) and obfuscated traffic" via "behavioural, statistical and heuristic analyses."97 AppLogic Networks (the rebranded Sandvine) publicly frames the same move — combining identification techniques to maintain classification "even as encryption and transports evolve" — and the integrated-collection specialists market the destination the value is migrating toward: Vehere positions "AI-powered Network Intelligence" with "100% Lossless packet capture," ClearTrail markets "MASS, TARGETED & TACTICAL Monitoring and Analysis" with a "CARBN.AI" module "for extracting intelligence from IP transactions."9899100My read. I read this as the front's defining technical migration: passive content interception is becoming passive behavioral intelligence. The reliable, durable yield of backbone collection is narrowing from plaintext toward metadata, traffic-pattern fingerprints, and subscriber correlation — exactly the wire-to-inference shift Front 3 sees at the endpoint, expressed here at national-network scale.The vendors that win the next cycle are the ones that reframe from content inspection to encrypted-traffic and behavioral intelligence, keeping their classification accuracy rising as the cleartext labels vanish. The ones still selling decryption-dependent inspection compress with the modality. The clearest public marker of who is on the right side of this is the DPI-engine layer itself. The ipoque R&S PACE 2 engine is embedded in other vendors' products, and its 2026 narrative is the single sharpest articulation of the problem the whole front now runs into.None of this is a claim that anyone reads encrypted content at scale; "classifies encrypted traffic" is an ML-fingerprinting positioning claim, not a content-visibility one, and the honest posture is to say so.Conditional prediction. Pattern Claim 2 — The Accountability & Sanctions Reset

Observation. The structure of who may sell mass-surveillance DPI across borders is being reshaped by sanctions and export-control accountability, on the public record end to end, and the most exposed vendor repositioned out of the intelligence framing entirely. The U.S. Department of Commerce added Sandvine Incorporated to the Entity List, published in the Federal Register on 27 February 2024. The determination was that its deep-packet-inspection products "were used to conduct mass web-monitoring and censorship and target human rights activists and dissidents."101 The commercial impact was material and self-disclosed: in September 2024 the company announced it would withdraw from 56 "non-democratic" countries, which it stated "represent approximately 45% of Sandvine's 2023 revenue."102 Commerce then removed Sandvine from the Entity List on 21 October 2024, with the official statement that "recognizing when a company has changed its behavior to protect national security and human rights is just as critical as restricting trade with parties of concern," after a "democracy-only" realignment.103 The company completed a debt restructuring, with a lender group taking control from prior owner Francisco Partners, and ~$430M owed to lenders reported as of December 2024. It then rebranded as AppLogic Networks in March 2025, whose current positioning is telecom-QoE "observability for network service providers," not intelligence collection.104105 The framing the regimes operate under is also public: the EU Dual-Use Regulation 2021/821 added an Article 5 catch-all reaching non-listed "cyber-surveillance items," importing a human-rights end-use test, with Commission guidelines issued 15 October 2024.106My read. I read this as a market-structure event, not a verdict on any one firm: the accountability regime is now a first-order force on the F1 supply side, shaping who can sell mass-collection technology and where. The mechanism is public, named, and dated. A single Entity-List designation was attached, on the company's own disclosure, to roughly 45% of its revenue base. The response was to exit repressive markets, restructure, rebrand, and reposition toward telecom observability and away from the intelligence framing altogether.Structurally, this concentrates the legitimate supply toward vendors who can clear export licensing and survive scrutiny, and pushes the most exposed modalities toward the regulatory and reputational margins. The Sandvine arc is the front's cleanest public proof of the mechanism, and it is a cured event: listing to removal to rebrand inside roughly a year. That is exactly why it belongs as a market-force datapoint rather than as a graded casualty.The regime is also not monotonic, and that is part of the read: pressure can ease as well as tighten, so this is an oscillating equilibrium, not a one-way ratchet.Conditional prediction. If, through 2026, the export-control regime keeps acting on mass-surveillance DPI — further Entity-List actions, EU Dual-Use Article 5 catch-all enforcement, or named-outlet documentation of completed market exits — the accountability overhang is a durable, supply-concentrating force, and the rebrand-and-reposition response repeats among the most exposed peers. If instead the regime visibly loosens, or AppLogic or a peer publicly re-enters the markets it exited without consequence, the overhang reads as a transient compliance episode rather than a structural reset, and the supply side stops concentrating toward the export-clearable end.Sources. Pattern Claim 3 — The Sovereign-Build Ceiling

Observation. The most capable mass-collection demand in this front is not addressable by any commercial vendor, because the most sophisticated buyers build rather than buy — and the public record describes the result at a scale no commercial product matches. XKEYSCORE has been documented since 2013 as the National Security Agency's "widest-reaching" system for "Digital Network Intelligence." I read the build-versus-buy fault line as the structural reason the F1 commercial universe is smaller than its demand. At the top of the buyer pyramid, the Five-Eyes-class agencies, the alternative to a vendor is the agency's own engineering org. The public record shows those programs operating at a reach the commercial market does not contest. That caps commercial penetration of the top tier structurally: the largest collection programs in the world are sovereign builds, public-anchored, and uncontested by any vendor in this chapter.The commercial market is therefore the addressable remainder — mid-tier national agencies without a sovereign build, allied services buying to a national-database integration, and the OEM engine layer that even some sovereign programs draw on.
