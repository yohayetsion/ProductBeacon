## 1. Brand Voice Definition

### 1.1 Voice Attributes

ProductBeacon's voice lives at the intersection of senior authority and approachable pragmatism. Four attributes define how we sound:

| Attribute | This | Not That |
|-----------|------|----------|
| **Grounded** | "We've seen this pattern across 50+ product orgs" | "Our innovative synergistic methodology..." |
| **Direct** | "Your roadmap has three problems. Here's how to fix them." | "Perhaps we might consider exploring potential areas of improvement..." |
| **Warm-Sharp** | Confident expertise delivered without ego or jargon | Cold consulting-speak or overly casual bro-culture |
| **Evidence-Led** | "This approach increased activation 3x for a Series B fintech" | "We believe in best practices and thought leadership" |

**The voice test:** Every piece of ProductBeacon copy should sound like a senior product leader talking to a peer over coffee. Not lecturing. Not selling. Sharing hard-won insight with someone they respect.

### 1.2 Tone Spectrum

The voice stays constant; the tone shifts by context:

| Context | Tone Calibration | Example |
|---------|-----------------|---------|
| **Website (hero/landing)** | Confident, aspirational, concise | "World-class product leadership. Your team. Your pace." |
| **Proposals/Decks** | Precise, structured, outcome-focused | "Phase 1 deliverables: validated positioning, competitive landscape, GTM strategy." |
| **Case Studies** | Narrative, evidence-rich, candid | "They had 40 features on the roadmap and no way to say no. Six weeks later, they had three strategic bets and a team that understood why." |
| **LinkedIn/Social** | Opinionated, personal, conversational | "Hot take: your product org doesn't need more process. It needs better decisions." |
| **Email (cold outreach)** | Respectful, specific, brief | "I noticed [specific thing]. I've helped three similar companies solve [specific problem]." |
| **Email (client comms)** | Warm, clear, action-oriented | "Here's the PRD. Two items need your call before Thursday." |
| **Blog/Thought Leadership** | Authoritative, teaching, generous | "Here's the framework I used to restructure a $200M product portfolio. Steal it." |

### 1.3 Writing Principles

1. **Lead with the outcome.** Don't describe what we do, describe what changes. "Go from roadmap chaos to strategic clarity" beats "We provide roadmap consulting services."

2. **Earn every sentence.** If a sentence doesn't add information or advance the reader's understanding, cut it. No filler. No preamble.

3. **Name the problem before the solution.** Prospects need to feel seen before they'll listen. Start with their pain, not our offering.

4. **Use specifics, not superlatives.** "17 years across NICE and Cognyte managing $200M+ portfolios" beats "extensive experience at leading companies."

5. **Write for the decision-maker, not the committee.** Our buyer is a CEO or VP. They scan, not read. Short paragraphs. Bold the important bits. Make it skimmable.

6. **Show the craft.** When we describe AI augmentation, show what it actually produces. Screenshots, sample deliverables, before/after. Don't just claim it.

## 2. Visual Identity Direction

### 2.1 Color Palette

The current purple (#B78DEB) and teal (#00E8DA) combination feels like a SaaS product template, not a premium services brand. The palette needs to communicate: seniority, warmth, precision, modernity.

**Recommended Direction: Deep Navy + Warm Amber**

This sits between AXIA's cool dark teal and Legionis's warm stone/amber, creating a distinct identity that feels like a natural sibling to both.

#### Primary Colors

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| **Primary Dark** | Deep Navy | `#0F172A` | Backgrounds, headers, primary surfaces |
| **Primary Mid** | Slate | `#1E293B` | Cards, elevated surfaces, secondary backgrounds |
| **Primary Accent** | Amber | `#F59E0B` | CTAs, highlights, emphasis, active states |

#### Secondary Colors

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| **Secondary Accent** | Warm White | `#FEF3C7` | Amber tint for subtle highlights on dark |
| **Signal Green** | Emerald | `#10B981` | Success states, positive metrics, "outcome achieved" |
| **Signal Blue** | Sky | `#38BDF8` | Links, informational elements, data viz secondary |
| **Warm Gray** | Slate 400 | `#94A3B8` | Body text on dark, secondary information |

#### Neutral Scale

| Name | Hex | Usage |
|------|-----|-------|
| **Ink** | `#0F172A` | Deepest background |
| **Surface** | `#1E293B` | Card/container backgrounds |
| **Border** | `#334155` | Dividers, borders, subtle separations |
| **Muted** | `#64748B` | Placeholder text, disabled states |
| **Secondary** | `#94A3B8` | Body text (dark mode), captions |
| **Primary Text** | `#F1F5F9` | Headings, primary text on dark |
| **Bright** | `#F8FAFC` | Maximum contrast text, hero headings |

#### Why This Palette

| Current (Being Replaced) | Proposed | Rationale |
|---------------------------|----------|-----------|
| Purple #B78DEB | Deep Navy #0F172A | Purple reads "SaaS startup" or "creative agency." Navy reads "senior, trustworthy, institutional." It's the color of authority without being cold. |
| Teal #00E8DA | Amber #F59E0B | Teal is everywhere in tech (AXIA owns that space already). Amber is warm, distinctive, and signals "premium" and "energy." It differentiates from the navy/blue consulting crowd. |
| Black background (flat) | Navy-to-Slate gradient | Adds depth and sophistication vs. flat black. Matches the multi-layer approach of AXIA/Legionis presentations. |

#### Relationship to Sibling Brands

| Brand | Primary BG | Accent | Temperature |
|-------|------------|--------|-------------|
| AXIA | #0D1117 (near-black) | #0D9488 (teal) | Cool |
| Legionis | #1C1917 (warm stone) | #D97706 (amber) | Warm |
| **ProductBeacon** | **#0F172A (deep navy)** | **#F59E0B (amber)** | **Warm-Neutral** |

ProductBeacon shares Legionis's amber warmth but with a navy foundation that feels more "executive services" than "platform product."

### 2.2 Typography

#### Recommended Pairing

| Role | Font | Weight | Rationale |
|------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Headings** | **Inter** | 600 (Semi-Bold), 700 (Bold) | Clean, professional, excellent readability at all sizes. More refined than Montserrat (current). Widely used in premium SaaS/tech but with a professional edge. Available on Google Fonts. |
| **Body** | **Inter** | 400 (Regular), 500 (Medium) | Single-family approach for consistency. Inter's large x-height makes body text highly readable. |
| **Code/Data** | **JetBrains Mono** | 400, 500 | Carries over from AXIA/Legionis for consistency. Used in deliverable previews, framework diagrams, metrics. |
| **Accent/Pull Quotes** | **Inter** | 300 (Light), Italic | For testimonials, callout text, differentiated emphasis. |

**Why Inter over Montserrat:** Montserrat is the default "startup" font. It's on every Wix template. Inter is equally accessible (Google Fonts, free) but reads as more intentional. Its optical sizing and tight metrics feel precision-engineered, which matches our "rigor" value.

**Why not Manrope (used by AXIA/Legionis):** Differentiation. ProductBeacon needs its own typographic identity. Inter is in the same clean-geometric family but distinct enough to feel like its own brand.

### 2.3 Photography & Imagery Direction

**The problem with current imagery:** Stock photos of aerial islands, motorcycle races, and babies with abacuses have zero connection to product leadership. They communicate "I picked from a template library."

**The replacement strategy:** No stock photography. Period.

| Instead of Stock Photos | Use This |
|-------------------------|----------|
| Generic team photos | **Actual deliverable screenshots** - real PRDs, roadmaps, frameworks |
| Abstract imagery | **Diagrammatic illustrations** - clean, branded system diagrams |
| Hero stock images | **Typographic heroes** - bold text with subtle geometric patterns |
| Testimonial headshots | **Company logos** (with permission) or anonymized case study visuals |
| Office/workspace shots | **Before/after views** of real artifacts (redacted client work) |

#### Imagery Principles

1. **Show the work.** The most compelling imagery for ProductBeacon is actual deliverables: a beautifully structured PRD slide, a strategic bet framework, a competitive landscape analysis. Screenshot them, brand them, use them.

2. **Geometric abstractions over photos.** Where visual filler is needed, use branded geometric patterns: intersecting grid lines, node graphs, beacon/lighthouse motifs rendered as clean line art.

3. **Data visualization as art.** Charts, frameworks, and matrices should be styled so beautifully they serve as both information and decoration. Think: the AXIA presentation aesthetic applied to consulting deliverables.

4. **Founder photography (limited).** One professional headshot of Yohay for the About page and LinkedIn. Shot in controlled lighting against a dark background. No stock-style poses.

### 2.4 Iconography Style

**Style:** Line icons, 1.5px stroke, rounded caps and joins.

**Color:** Primary text color (#F1F5F9) with amber (#F59E0B) accent on hover/active.

**Set:** Custom or curated subset from Lucide Icons (open source, same family as Radix/shadcn). Lucide is clean, consistent, and developer-friendly.

**Key icons needed:**

| Concept | Icon Reference |
|---------|---------------|
| Strategy/Vision | Compass, Target |
| Product/Build | Layers, Blocks |
| AI/Augmentation | Sparkles, Cpu |
| Data/Analytics | BarChart3, TrendingUp |
| Team/Org | Users, Network |
| Speed/Delivery | Zap, Clock |
| Quality/Craft | Award, CheckCircle |

### 2.5 Layout Principles

1. **Generous whitespace.** Premium brands breathe. Minimum 80px vertical padding between sections. Let content anchor to a clear grid with space around it.

2. **Left-aligned asymmetry.** Avoid centered everything (that's the template look). Primary text anchors left. Supporting visuals or data anchor right. Creates visual tension and movement.

3. **Content width: 720px max for prose.** Long lines kill readability. Cap paragraph width at 720px (roughly 75 characters per line). Allow full-width for visuals and data grids.

4. **Vertical rhythm.** Consistent spacing multiplier: 8px base unit. All spacing is a multiple of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96, 128).

5. **Section contrast.** Alternate between deep navy (#0F172A) and slate (#1E293B) backgrounds to create visual sections without heavy dividers.

### 3.4 Dark Mode vs. Light Mode

**Recommendation: Dark-first, with light mode consideration.**

**Primary experience:** Dark mode. This aligns with the founder's aesthetic (AXIA and Legionis are both dark), communicates technical sophistication, and differentiates from the corporate light-mode consulting world.

**Light mode:** Not required for V1 launch. If added later, invert the palette:
- Background: #F8FAFC (Bright) and #F1F5F9
- Cards: White (#FFFFFF) with #E2E8F0 borders
- Text: #0F172A (Ink) for headings, #334155 for body
- Accent: #D97706 (slightly darker amber for light backgrounds)

**When to consider light mode:** If/when ProductBeacon produces PDF proposals or printed materials. PDFs are better in light mode for ink economy and readability.

### 3.5 Responsive Design Principles

1. **Mobile-optimized, desktop-designed.** The site will be built responsively, but the primary experience is desktop. ProductBeacon's buyers (CEOs, VPs) typically browse on desktop during work hours.

2. **No horizontal scroll. Ever.** Tables, code blocks, and frameworks must wrap or scroll internally.

3. **Touch targets: 44px minimum.** Buttons, links, and interactive elements must be at least 44x44px on mobile.

4. **Progressive disclosure on mobile.** Collapse detailed sections (framework breakdowns, deliverable lists) behind expandable headers. Don't force vertical marathon scrolling.

5. **Images: Full-bleed on mobile.** Deliverable screenshots and diagrams go edge-to-edge on small screens to maximize readability.

## 6. Logo Direction

### 6.1 Concept Directions

#### Direction A: The Beacon Mark

A simplified lighthouse/beacon form. Not a literal lighthouse, but an abstracted geometric mark suggesting upward-directed light or signal.

**Visual concept:** A vertical bar (the tower) with radiating angular lines at the top (the light). Clean, geometric, minimal strokes.

**What it communicates:** Guidance, direction, signal through noise, illumination. Directly connects to the "Beacon" in ProductBeacon.

**Personality:** Trustworthy, clear, purposeful.

**Execution:** Works as a standalone mark at small sizes (favicon, social avatar). Amber mark on navy, or white mark on navy.

#### Direction B: The Constellation Mark

A network of connected dots forming an upward arrow or ascending pattern. Think: constellation or node graph that resolves into a directional shape.

**Visual concept:** 5-7 dots connected by thin lines, forming an abstract upward-pointing shape. Not a literal arrow, more like a constellation that implies direction.

**What it communicates:** Connected thinking, pattern recognition, strategic vision, a path becoming clear.

**Personality:** Modern, intelligent, systematic.

**Execution:** Works well animated (dots connecting in sequence for web). Static version is clean and simple.

#### Direction C: The PB Monogram

A custom letterform combining P and B into a single, distinctive mark.

**Visual concept:** The vertical stroke of P shared with B, creating a unified letterform. Geometric, with rounded elements that reference the beacon concept (the curves of P and B suggesting the rounded top of a lighthouse).

**What it communicates:** Professional, established, personal brand with institutional feel.

**Personality:** Classic-modern, confident, premium.

**Execution:** Extremely versatile (works at any size, any color, any background). Strong favicon. Clear brand ownership.

### 6.2 Recommendation

**Direction A (The Beacon Mark)** as primary recommendation. Reasons:

1. **Name connection:** It makes the brand name visual. People will see the mark and think "beacon" without effort.
2. **Distinctiveness:** No major competitor in this space uses a beacon/lighthouse mark. Differentiation is immediate.
3. **Scalability:** Works from 16px favicon to full-page hero.
4. **Flexibility:** Can be rendered in amber-on-navy (primary), white-on-navy (reversed), or navy-on-white (light contexts).

**Direction C (Monogram)** as fallback if the beacon mark proves too literal or too similar to existing lighthouse logos in other industries.

### 6.3 Logo System

| Variant | Usage |
|---------|-------|
| **Full logo** | Mark + "ProductBeacon" wordmark. Primary usage on website, proposals, decks. |
| **Mark only** | Favicon, social avatar, small spaces. |
| **Wordmark only** | Text contexts where the mark doesn't fit (email signatures, footers). |

**Wordmark typography:** "Product" in Inter 400, "Beacon" in Inter 700. No space between words. This creates a visual emphasis on "Beacon" while "Product" provides context.

### 6.4 Logo Do's and Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use on navy or dark backgrounds | Place on busy photography |
| Maintain clear space (1x mark height on all sides) | Stretch, rotate, or distort |
| Use amber, white, or navy color variants | Use in colors outside the palette |
| Scale proportionally | Add effects (shadows, gradients, glows) |
| Let it breathe with whitespace | Crowd it with other elements |
| Use the mark alone when space is tight | Use the wordmark alone at small sizes |
