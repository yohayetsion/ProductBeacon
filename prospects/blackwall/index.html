THE MARKET

Bot management is riding a $700M wave toward $2.5B.

Bot management is a roughly $700M to $900M segment in 2024, with forecasts reaching $2.5B by 2030 at a 22-25% CAGR (Markets and Markets, Mordor Intelligence). The broader Web Application Firewall layer sits at $7-8B heading to $22-25B by 2030-2032 (Grand View, Markets and Markets).

Three forces are reshaping this category right now. AI-driven scraping by LLM bots from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google has opened an attack surface visible since 2023 that most existing WAF rulesets were not designed for. Enterprise-tier WAF is commoditizing fast, while the SMB long tail stays structurally under-served by pricing. Regulatory pressure from NIS2, DORA, and SEC cyber disclosure is pushing bot and DDoS posture onto board agendas. That tailwind lifts every vendor but disproportionately rewards channel partners who can package compliance as a margin line.

YOUR MOAT

Three differentiators mega-vendors cannot copy on a roadmap.

01

Channel-native product architecture

GateKeeper is engineered to be embedded inside a hosting provider's traffic layer and resold, not bolted on through a partner program. Cloudflare, DataDome, and HUMAN cannot replicate this without cannibalizing their direct-to-customer revenue. The conflict is structural, not tactical.

02

On-premises deployment with reverse-proxy stickiness

Security integrated at the traffic entry point becomes operationally load-bearing. Ripping it out means rearchitecting traffic flow. Cloud-distributed competitors architecturally cannot serve EU-sovereign, regulated, or data-residency-constrained hosting providers. Switching cost is baked into infrastructure, not into a contract.

03

Control-panel distribution footprint

cPanel and Plesk integrations place Blackwall inside the default admin surface of a large share of SMB websites globally. Every hosting partner added compounds the integration depth. 2.3M+ sites protected and 30-40% revenue uplift per end-customer for hosting partners are the numbers that moat actually produces (company figures).

RECOMMENDATIONS

Four moves the next 12 months will reward.

1

Rewrite the hero around partner economics, not audience label.

Replace "Designed for Service & Hosting Providers" with a partner-P&L claim. Move the 2.3M-site proof and the 30-40% uplift into the hero. Make the CFO-closing line the first line, not the fifth scroll. Demote GateKeeper feature content to a dedicated product page.

2

Build the multi-product portfolio architecture before the next SKU ships.

The Series B announcement hinted at "additional infrastructure and security products," but the brand architecture (Blackwall-the-platform, GateKeeper-the-product, future products under what naming convention) is not visible. Define it now, before the anti-LLM-scraper SKU and whatever follows land as loose features.

3

Launch a published entry tier for MSPs and smaller hosting providers.

Channel-negotiated pricing is correct for the DigitalOcean and Vultr tier of partner. It leaves MSPs and smaller regional hosts unable to self-serve evaluation, and it lets Cloudflare anchor on "free to start" with no counter-anchor. A lightweight published tier closes both gaps without collapsing channel economics.

4

Ship a US/APAC partner-enablement layer as a product, not a campaign.

The channel economics that work in Europe will not translate cleanly to the US, where Sucuri's GoDaddy distribution is entrenched and hosting is fragmented, or to APAC, where Tencent Cloud and regional ISPs require distinct partner motions. Battlecards, ROI calculators, co-branded content, and partner-tier onboarding are product work now, not marketing collateral.

THE BIGGER PICTURE

The window to own this category is real, and narrow.

The category is consolidating around enterprise WAF bundles while a handful of direct-to-SMB players (Sucuri via GoDaddy, Cloudflare via free tier) fight for the long tail.
