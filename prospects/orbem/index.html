orbem. Product Strategy Brief

You built a monopoly. Your homepage still sells a miracle.

A look at positioning, moat, and the product-organization moves that turn EUR 55.5M of Series B fuel into category ownership.

The Market

Three markets stacked on one platform, each on a different clock.

In-ovo sexing is regulatory-forced and moving fast. EU penetration doubled from 20% to 28% in twelve months (Innovate Animal Ag, April 2025). Germany banned male-chick culling in 2022, France in 2023, Italy followed, and Norway and Switzerland commit to full transition by 2025 to 2027. The US, historically closed, opened in late 2024 under Walmart's 2025 supplier guidance, Unilever commitments, and United Egg Producers certification. That shift made the US the category's biggest land-grab, on a window measured in quarters, not years.

Underneath the same AI-MRI stack sit two larger horizons: non-destructive fruit and vegetable grading (Orbem's own framing cites 1.25B tonnes of produce lost between harvest and retail) and long-horizon industrial MRI for preventive human health. Three markets, three buying cycles, one product organization.

Competitive Landscape

A four-vendor fight with two dark horses.

Orbem is the only non-invasive, both-layer (brown and white), imaging-based vendor already deployed at commercial scale, with 9 European hatcheries plus US installations and distribution through Vencomatic Group. Behind AAT on price and throughput. Ahead of Seleggt on invasiveness. In a dead heat with MatrixSpec for the US white-egg race, and MatrixSpec has not yet shipped.

Your Moat

Three strategic advantages, in order of durability.

01

Industrial MRI reinvented for biological throughput

Every competitor solves sexing with a different physics stack. Orbem took a clinical modality with a fifty-year history of being slow and expensive, re-engineered it for sub-second inline scanning, and put it on hatchery floors. A well-funded competitor needs five to seven years to reach parity on the physics, AI, and automation stack combined.

Durability: very high
02

The world's largest biological MRI training dataset

170 million eggs scanned compounds into model accuracy no entrant can bootstrap. The same imaging primitives transfer to fruits, nuts, and eventually human tissue.

Durability: compounding and irreversible
03

Proprietary claim on fertilization-status detection (Genus Scale)

No named competitor has a product in this sub-category. It applies to every egg, not only table eggs, so its addressable base is arguably larger than in-ovo sexing itself.

Durability: medium to high, protected by MRI stack plus dataset

What's working

The Genus product architecture (Focus, Scale, Echo) signals platform, not point solution. The Technology page's concession-rebuttal on MRI's reputation is rhetorically strong.

What's not

The hero does not pick a buyer. The market is rewarding operational proof (deployment count, accuracy, day-of-detection), not philosophy. "Invisible visible" is a mission sentence forced to carry a hero's job.

Value Prop Gap

From modality marketing to operational asset.

Current framing
"Determine sex and fertility non-invasively. AI-powered MRI uncovers what is hidden deep beneath the surface."

This is modality marketing. It tells a hatchery operator what the box does, not what changes in their P&L on Monday morning. "Non-invasive" and "fast" are spec language in a category where AAT is cheaper and Seleggt offers a lease.

Reframed
"Every egg you load becomes a data point. You run a fuller incubator, hit welfare commitments your retailer audits, and price pullets against buyers who now pay more for traceability."

The shift: from "we see inside the egg" to "every egg becomes an asset in your operational, regulatory, and commercial posture." Pricing anchors to the value of the data stream, not machine economics.

Why the gap matters: Orbem gets compared line-by-line with AAT on price and throughput (a losing frame), the fruits and healthcare arcs read as unrelated verticals rather than one compounding platform, and pricing stays anchored to machine economics instead of the value of the data stream.

Narrative Opportunity

One story for everyone, or three stories that compound into one.

Today

Orbem tells one story to everyone: "the invisible visible with AI-powered MRI for everything and everyone." A hatchery COO, a fruit packer, and a preventive-health investor meet the same sentence and none are fully served.

In a category where the third-party scorekeeper (Innovate Animal Ag) ranks vendors by installed hatcheries and per-pullet cost, "the invisible visible" cedes ground to vendors whose language is tighter to the buyer's math.

Tomorrow

Orbem tells three stories that compound into one. US poultry buyers meet a page built on white-layer coverage and the 170M-egg proof. Fruits and health meet platform vision and dataset asymmetry. The mission stays the same. The hero does not.

The Series B window is the repositioning window. Funding four motions in parallel without splitting the narrative forces one homepage to carry four buyer economics at once.

That is the moment to pick the buyer for each motion and let the platform story sit behind them, not in front of them.

Recommendations

Four moves before the US window closes.

01

Lead the US poultry narrative with white-layer coverage.

In US-facing content, "the only non-invasive in-ovo solution that works for white eggs" is the headline, not a spec bullet. Pair it with the 170M-eggs scale proof in the hero. Brown-only competitors (AAT, Nectra) are structurally unable to serve the commodity US market. This is the one line that wins the US window before MatrixSpec ships.

02

Give Genus Scale its own category-creation narrative.

A monopoly product deserves monopoly positioning. Today Scale is co-presented with Focus under "Poultry" at equal weight. Split it: a fertilization-detection product line with its own page, its own economic story (yield, not welfare), and its own buyer (operations director, not sustainability lead). The addressable base is larger than in-ovo sexing itself.

03

Package for both buying moments.

Default to integrated equipment sale leaves mid-size hatcheries under Walmart or UEP pressure with nowhere to go but Seleggt's lease. Add a per-egg lease motion for the Welfare Compliance buyer. Keep the CapEx motion for the Hatchery Yield buyer. Two packages, two product requirement sets, same platform.

04

Split product leadership by buying motion, not by vertical.

Inside poultry alone, name two commercial motions: Welfare Compliance (sold to CEO and sustainability, per-egg lease) and Hatchery Yield (sold to operations, throughput and capacity tied to Genus Scale). Fruits and healthcare each get a discovery-mode product lead with a productization-versus-research charter, not a shipping roadmap. This is an organizational change, not a rebrand.

The Bigger Picture

The Series B clock is running.

EUR 55.5M committed in January 2026 to four motions (US scale, poultry manufacturing capacity, fruits entry via Genus Echo, healthcare R&D) means every quarter without a per-motion buyer definition is a quarter that defaults into the old "invisible visible" story. The US poultry window is the shortest of the four. MatrixSpec's Day 4 claim, if it commercializes, collapses the welfare and incubator-capacity story at exactly the wrong moment.

A company that has built a category of one and the world's largest biological MRI dataset does not have a technology problem. It has a narrative and packaging problem, and a product-organization question underneath it: who owns the commercial motion for each buyer, and what does their first 90 days look like.
