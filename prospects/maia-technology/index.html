THE MARKET

A replacement cycle the incumbents cannot stall

The global buy-side investment management software market sits at roughly $5 to $5.5 billion in 2024, compounding in the mid-teens toward $11 to $13 billion by 2030 (Mordor Intelligence, Grand View Research).

The replacement cycle is real: compressed fund fees, multi-asset mandates, T+1 settlement in the US since May 2024, and regulatory load from MiFID II, SFDR, and EMIR are forcing buyers off platforms installed in the 2000s and 2010s.

Consolidation is accelerating the trade. Deutsche Börse bought SimCorp for €3.9 billion in 2024. Clearwater is acquiring Enfusion. State Street owns Charles River. SS&C absorbed Eze in 2019. For a boutique or mid-market buyer, independence itself has become a pitch, and MAIA sits in the narrow window where buyers actively want an independent cloud-native alternative before the next roll-up takes it away.

YOUR MOAT

Three advantages competitors cannot retrofit

01

Real-time, event-driven IBOR

No refresh button, no batch process. Every incumbent IBOR (Geneva, Dimension, Eze, Charles River, Aladdin's older modules) is batch or refresh-driven. This is a founding architectural decision competitors cannot retrofit without a full platform rewrite they will not justify while collecting rent on installed base. Durability: five plus years.

02

A decade of production use inside an asset manager

The data models, workflows, and default configurations were shaped by people who ran portfolios, not engineers guessing at buy-side reality. Bloomberg, Enfusion, and Limina cannot manufacture this, because it is time and context, not code. Durability: permanent, as long as the story is actually told.

03

A quant-ready streaming API across Python, Java, C#, and Golang

Unconstrained bi-directional integration in four languages is rare at this scale. For systematic managers who build in-house, this is a direct invitation to program against the platform in the language they already write in.
