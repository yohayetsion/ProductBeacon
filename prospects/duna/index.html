Product Strategy Brief

Network Effects on Paper Only

A $35M Series A compliance platform with fintech royalty on the cap table has a "Business Identity Network" vision that could redefine onboarding. The question is whether network effects are being built into the product or exist only in the fundraising deck.

02/10

The Market

The compliance infrastructure market is growing on two converging forces. Regulatory complexity is expanding globally, with every new jurisdiction adding KYC, KYB, and AML requirements that compound the cost of onboarding businesses across borders. And AI is simultaneously creating new compliance risks (deepfakes, synthetic identities) and new solutions (automated document verification, AI-powered case management).

Compliance is shifting from cost center to product opportunity. Embedded finance requires embedded compliance. Cross-border commerce requires cross-border identity. The fintechs, banks, and platforms that need to onboard businesses compliantly are spending more each year.

Duna's $35M Series A from CapitalG, with angels from Snowflake, Adyen, Stripe, Notion, Personio, Wise, Mollie, Remote, Goldman Sachs, and Anthropic, validates both the market and the team. But the KYB/KYC/AML category has over ten well-funded competitors, and the buyer has seen the "AI-native compliance" pitch before.

03/10

04/10

Your Moat

Duna has three potential moat sources, each at a different maturity level.

European Regulatory Depth (Real Today). With 210+ local registries, UBO registers, and local VAT integration across 7+ languages, Duna has invested heavily in the compliance complexity that makes European KYB harder than US KYB. Middesk cannot replicate this easily.

Compliance as Code (Partially Built). The Policy Engine turns compliance policy into executable code. If policies become code on Duna's platform, switching costs increase dramatically because moving means re-encoding every policy from scratch.

Business Identity Network (Aspirational). If businesses onboarded through Duna can share their verified identity with other Duna customers, the network effect creates a moat no single-tenant tool can match. This is the moat that determines whether Duna becomes infrastructure or just another tool.

07/10

Narrative Opportunity

Today, Duna tells two stories that do not converge. The website sells compliance automation. The Conversations video series sells infrastructure. Both are good. They need to become one.

Tomorrow, the unified narrative: compliance is not a workflow to automate. It is a system to encode. Duna's Policy Engine turns compliance policy into executable code. That code runs across onboarding, decisioning, and lifecycle monitoring. And as more businesses are verified through that code, the Business Identity Network makes onboarding a one-click event.

That narrative takes the operational buyer on a journey from "this tool saves us time" to "this platform encodes our compliance DNA." It also answers the strategic risk: if Stripe or Adyen build compliance into their own platforms, they can replicate features, but they cannot replicate a network of shared business identities.

08/10

Four Moves

Move 1

Elevate the Policy Engine. Move it from module six to the architectural headline. "Compliance as code" is the most defensible positioning in the set. It should lead the homepage.

Move 2

Restructure the messaging hierarchy. Use the metrics (10.6x, 37%, 4.8x) as proof points beneath "compliance as code." The metrics sell a tool. The Policy Engine sells a platform.

Move 3

Define the network activation strategy now. Which customer segment creates the highest density of shared identity lookups? Build network effects at 60 people, before Series B growth pressure makes it impossible.

Move 4

Bridge the two narratives. Align every page to the infrastructure story (Conversations series), with tool-level detail as supporting evidence, not the other way around.

09/10

The Bigger Picture

Duna's angel roster reads like a blueprint for the problem it solves. Stripe, Adyen, Wise, Mollie: these are the companies whose growth was constrained by the compliance complexity Duna aims to eliminate. That is not just investor validation. It is a product thesis embedded in the cap table.

But the same investors represent a strategic risk. If Stripe or Adyen decide to build rather than partner, Duna's market position becomes complicated. The Business Identity Network vision is the strategic answer, because a network of shared identities is something no single platform can replicate.

The question is whether network effects are being architected into the product today, or whether they remain a slide in the fundraising deck. Post-Series A, the disciplined move is adoption density in one segment before geographic breadth. Network effects require density, not coverage.
