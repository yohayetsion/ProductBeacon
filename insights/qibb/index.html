Media Technology

qibb

Media workflow orchestration built on Node-RED with 3 of the 4 top US broadcasters as customers. The product is becoming an industry standard; the messaging still reads like a generic integration platform.

Market Context

The media workflow orchestration space sits at the intersection of two growing markets: broadcast automation software (valued at ~$3.1B in 2026, growing at 18.4% CAGR to $6B by 2030) and the broader workflow orchestration market ($21.9B in 2026, 13.3% CAGR).

Three macro forces are driving demand: the cloud migration of broadcast infrastructure, the explosion of content formats requiring automated multiplatform distribution, and AI/ML integration for metadata tagging, highlight generation, and content personalization. Media organizations increasingly need an orchestration layer that connects specialized tools without requiring deep custom development for every integration.

Competitive Landscape

qibb competes in a specific niche: low-code workflow orchestration purpose-built for media. Its true competitors are not generic automation tools (n8n, monday.com) but media technology incumbents.

Dalet Galaxy five is the closest functional competitor: MAM combined with workflow orchestration, but it is a heavyweight platform that locks customers into its ecosystem. Imagine Communications focuses on playout automation and ad management. Grass Valley is hardware-rooted, moving to software. None of the incumbents offer qibb's low-code, vendor-agnostic integration approach.

What makes qibb defensible

  • Media-native connector moat. 100+ pre-built connectors for Avid MediaCentral, Frame.io, Iconik, Mimir, and other media-specific systems. A horizontal iPaaS player would need years to build equivalent depth.
  • Node-RED ecosystem leverage. 5,000+ community nodes for free while layering proprietary media nodes on top. Massive connector breadth without requiring a 200-person engineering team.
  • Hybrid deployment for compliance-sensitive broadcast. Cloud-native with Kubernetes-based hybrid model and ISO 27001 for broadcasters with real on-premise requirements.

Positioning & Messaging

A- Believability. "3 of the 4 leading U.S. broadcasters" is a powerful specificity claim. Named logos and quantified outcomes (40% cost reduction, 10x faster builds) add credibility. The social proof creates a "who's NOT using this?" dynamic.

B+ Clarity. "Orchestrate media workflows smarter, faster, with less code" communicates the what clearly. But "orchestrate" is an engineering word. Media ops teams think in terms of "automate" or "connect."

B- Differentiation. The "vendor and partner agnostic" claim is the strongest differentiator but it is buried below the fold. The homepage leads with generic benefit language that could describe any iPaaS tool.

The core tension: qibb is becoming the de facto orchestration standard for broadcast, but the messaging does not claim that position. Against horizontal workflow tools, qibb wins on domain depth but does not make the contrast sharp. Against media incumbents, qibb's vendor-agnostic angle should be headline-level: "The Switzerland of media orchestration."

Strategic Recommendations

1. Stop selling a tool, start selling the standard

With 3 of 4 US broadcasters, qibb has a credible claim to being the de facto orchestration layer for broadcast. The connector moat (100+ media-specific integrations) is the hardest thing to replicate. Every new connector widens the gap. The messaging should reflect a platform that is becoming an industry standard, not a product competing for attention.

2. Resolve the PLG vs. enterprise sales tension

The website simultaneously signals "try before you buy" (product-led growth) and "book a demo" (enterprise sales). At 27 employees selling to risk-averse broadcasters, commit to consultative sales with a proof-of-concept model. Trying to run both motions with a team this size dilutes execution on both.
